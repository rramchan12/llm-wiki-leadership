## Quick summary
Why do some ideas stick while equally important ones are forgotten? The Heaths identify six principles (SUCCESs) that make messages memorable and persuasive: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories. The book provides a practical toolkit for leaders to communicate ideas that people remember and act on.

## Core thesis
The "Curse of Knowledge" — once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it — is the primary barrier to effective communication. Leaders fail to communicate because they operate at an abstraction level their audience can't access. The six SUCCESs principles counter this curse by grounding ideas in simplicity, surprise, concreteness, credibility, emotion, and narrative.

## Key frameworks / models

### The SUCCESs Framework
1. **Simple:** Find the core — strip the idea to its essential message. Not "dumbing down" — finding the single most important thing. "Commander's Intent" in the military.
2. **Unexpected:** Break patterns to get attention (surprise), then hold it (interest). Use curiosity gaps — create questions before providing answers.
3. **Concrete:** Make abstract ideas tangible. Use sensory language, real examples, concrete images. "A bird in the hand" beats "an advantageous situation."
4. **Credible:** Use external credibility (authorities, statistics) and internal credibility (vivid details, the Sinatra Test — "if I can make it there, I'll make it anywhere," testable credentials).
5. **Emotional:** People care about people, not numbers. Make them feel something. Appeal to identity ("What would someone like me do?") rather than self-interest alone.
6. **Stories:** Stories are flight simulators for the brain. They inspire action through simulation (how to act) and inspiration (the motivation to act). Three core plots: Challenge, Connection, Creativity.

### The Curse of Knowledge
The fundamental communication problem: experts can't remember what it was like not to know. They communicate in abstractions that make sense to them but not to their audience. Every SUCCESs principle is a tool to overcome this curse.

### Idea Clinics
Applying the SUCCESs framework to real-world communication problems: corporate strategy statements, nonprofit pitches, product descriptions. Systematic analysis of why messages fail and how to fix them.

## Chapter-level summaries

### Introduction: What Sticks?
- The kidney-heist urban legend vs. the average corporate memo. Why urban legends spread and memos don't. The six principles framework.

### Ch 1: Simple
- Commander's Intent: the core idea that survives when the plan falls apart. Southwest Airlines: "THE low-fare airline." Inverted pyramid journalism.

### Ch 2: Unexpected
- Nordstrom service legends that surprise and stick. Curiosity gaps: the Roone Arledge approach to sports broadcasting. Gap theory of curiosity (Loewenstein).

### Ch 3: Concrete
- The Asian velvet vs. the concrete details in Aesop's fables. HP's "names, dates, and places" rule.

### Ch 4: Credible
- The Sinatra Test. Anti-authority endorsements (reformed smokers). Testable credentials ("see for yourself").

### Ch 5: Emotional
- Mother Teresa: "If I look at the mass, I will never act." Semantic stretch and why overused words lose emotional punch. Identity appeals.

### Ch 6: Stories
- Jared from Subway. The three plots: Challenge (David vs. Goliath), Connection (Good Samaritan), Creativity (the apple-from-a-tree moment). Stories as mental simulation.

### Epilogue: What Sticks
- Putting it together. The checklist approach. Spotting ideas that are naturally sticky.

## Cross-book connections
- **Switch (Heath):** "Find the feeling" (Motivate the Elephant) maps directly to the Emotional principle. "Script the critical moves" maps to Simple and Concrete. "Point to the destination" is a sticky destination postcard.
- **Decisive (Heath):** The WRAP framework helps leaders *decide* well; Made to Stick helps them *communicate* those decisions so they actually land.
- **Radical Candor (Scott):** Giving feedback that sticks requires the SUCCESs principles — especially Concrete (specific examples, not abstractions) and Emotional (making the person feel cared about).
- **The First 90 Days (Watkins):** Early wins must be communicated to stick. A new leader who can't explain their vision in sticky terms fails to build the coalition they need.
- **The Power of Moments (Heath):** Defining moments are inherently sticky — they combine Unexpectedness, Emotion, and Concrete sensory detail.
