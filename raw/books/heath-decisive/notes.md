# Decisive: How to Make Better Choices in Life and Work
## Chip Heath & Dan Heath — 2013

---

### Core Argument
Most bad decisions aren't the result of stupidity — they're the result of a predictable set of cognitive biases. The Heaths identify four "villains" of decision-making and a four-step framework (WRAP) to counter each villain.

---

### The Four Villains of Decision-Making

1. **Narrow Framing** — We define options too narrowly, often as binary (should I do X or not?). The real question is: what are all the ways I could approach this?
2. **Confirmation Bias** — We seek out information that confirms what we already believe, ignoring disconfirming evidence. We turn decisions into self-fulfilling prophecies.
3. **Short-Term Emotion** — We get caught up in how we feel right now, which distorts our judgment. The 10-minute test: "How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?"
4. **Overconfidence** — We think we know more about how the future will unfold than we actually do. We under-plan for failure.

---

### The WRAP Framework

**W — Widen Your Options**
- Escape narrow framing by generating more choices
- The "vanishing options" test: If none of your current options were available, what would you do?
- Multi-tracking: Pursue multiple options simultaneously (even as a deliberate experiment)
- Find someone who has already solved this problem (look for bright spots)
- Ladder up/down: What's the underlying goal? What's the smallest possible step?

**R — Reality-Test Your Assumptions**
- Fight confirmation bias with active information-seeking
- "What would have to be true for this option to be right?" (flip the lens)
- Seek disconfirming information: Who disagrees? Why?
- Ooching: Small experiments before full commitment (test your assumptions cheaply)
- Talk to people who have direct experience (base rates, expert opinion)
- The "10,000-hour" reality check on new ventures

**A — Attain Distance Before Deciding**
- Overcome short-term emotion with perspective
- 10/10/10: How will you feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
- Ask: "What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"
- Clarify your core priorities BEFORE emotions cloud the decision
- The "honor your core priorities" exercise: What would you regret NOT doing?
- Andy Grove's "through the window" test: Imagine a new CEO walking in — what would they do?

**P — Prepare to Be Wrong**
- Counter overconfidence with mental simulation
- Premortem: "It's one year from now. This decision failed spectacularly. What happened?"
- Pre-parade: "It's one year from now. This worked brilliantly. What made it succeed?"
- Set tripwires: "If X happens, we will revisit this decision." Build in automatic checkpoints.
- Bookend the future: imagine both disaster scenario and breakthrough scenario
- Place small bets to limit downside while preserving upside

---

### Key Examples

- **Quaker / Snapple acquisition** — $1.7B acquisition, sold 3 years later for $300M. Classic confirmation bias: ignored signals that didn't fit the bullish narrative.
- **Intel's pivot from memory chips to microprocessors** — Andy Grove's "through the window" test. What would a new CEO do? Freed them from the status quo trap.
- **The Steve Cole longevity research** — People with a "eudaimonic" sense of purpose live longer. Core priorities matter beyond productivity.
- **The Intertek engineer** — Ooched her way into a new career through small experiments, not a leap of faith.
- **The nurse and the newborn** — Trusted her gut when something felt wrong; an outlier case where emotion (expert intuition) overrode protocol and saved a life.

### Key Quotes

- "A remarkable aspect of your mental life is that you are rarely stumped." — Kahneman (cited)
- "The single biggest problem with decision-making is not that we are short on data — it's that we are long on self-justification."
- "What would I tell my best friend to do?" — The emotion-distance technique.
- "If in doubt, don't." — Buffett's tripwire.
- "Decisions are made in the now, but consequences are lived in the later."
