# The One Minute Manager — Kenneth Blanchard & Spencer Johnson (1982)

**One-liner:** Great management distills to three one-minute interactions — goal-setting, praising, and redirection — that let people feel good about themselves and produce good results.

## Core Thesis

Management is too complex only because we make it so. Most managers oscillate between two extremes: autocratic managers who produce results but at the cost of morale, and permissive managers who produce happy people but mediocre performance. The One Minute Manager demonstrates a third way — practical, brief, and both humane and effective.

The philosophy: "People who feel good about themselves produce good results." The three management practices operationalize this: set expectations clearly, immediately reinforce what's right, and immediately correct what's wrong — all in behavioral, not personal, terms.

The book uses a parable format: a young man searches for a good manager, eventually finding and learning from "The One Minute Manager."

## Key Frameworks

### The Three Secrets (One Minute Practices)

#### Secret 1: One Minute Goals

- **At the start of a role or project**, agree on goals that can be written on a single page in less than 250 words
- Each goal statement: describe the goal, describe the expected standard of performance
- The employee re-reads and re-states the goals — ensuring real understanding
- Keep the written goals visible; refer to them regularly
- Check behavior against goals constantly

**Why it works:** Most performance problems are goal-setting problems — people don't know what is expected. Clarity removes ambiguity, creates accountability, and enables self-management.

#### Secret 2: One Minute Praisings

1. Tell the person immediately what they did right (specific, behavioral)
2. Tell them how you feel about it (genuine, personal)
3. Pause — let the praise sink in
4. Encourage them to do more of the same

**Principle:** Catch people doing things right, not wrong. Most managers manage by exception — they only speak up when something goes wrong. One Minute Praisings redirect that attention. Immediate and specific feedback is far more effective than annual reviews.

#### Secret 3: One Minute Reprimands (Redirects)

1. **First half:** Tell the person what they did wrong — immediately, specifically. Tell them how you feel about it. Pause.
2. **Second half:** Remind them that you value them; clarify it's the behavior — not the person — that is the problem. Affirm your confidence in them.

The reprimand is over the moment it ends — do not carry it over. "A reprimand is not stored up and then delivered — it is given once and then forgotten."

**Key distinction from typical management:** Most managers let minor transgressions slide until they're so frustrated they deliver a prolonged attack. One Minute Reprimands prevent accumulation.

### The People-Results Balance

```
       Results
         ↑
         |
Good Mgmt●
         |
         |______→ People
```

The insight that managers who focus exclusively on results (Theory X) may get short-term performance but destroy trust. Managers who focus exclusively on people (Theory Y permissiveness) build trust but allow drift. The One Minute Manager achieves both by treating people as responsible adults who deserve clear expectations and honest feedback.

### The New One Minute Manager (2015 Update)

Blanchard updated the model to replace "One Minute Reprimands" with **One Minute Re-Directs**, emphasizing that modern management should focus on redirecting behavior through collaborative coaching rather than blame-based correction. The re-direct also acknowledges when the manager failed to set clear enough goals.
