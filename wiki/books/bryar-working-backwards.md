# Working Backwards — Colin Bryar & Bill Carr (2021)

**One-liner:** Amazon's operational excellence comes not from culture slogans but from a set of rigorously enforced mechanisms — Working Backwards, Bar Raiser hiring, six-page narratives, and 14 Leadership Principles — that force clarity, customer obsession, and long-term thinking into every decision.

## Core Thesis

Most companies try to copy Amazon's *outcomes* (speed, innovation, customer centricity) without copying the *mechanisms* that produce them. Bryar and Carr, both longtime Amazon executives, argue that Amazon's success is not a mystery: it is the result of deliberate, sometimes counterintuitive operating choices — chief among them the PR-FAQ method, the six-page narrative format, the Bar Raiser hiring bar, and the Leadership Principles used as an actual decision-making operating system, not a wall poster.

The central insight: **start from the customer experience you want to create, then work backwards to the product/service/process that delivers it.** This inverts the typical innovation flow (start from what we can build → see if customers want it) and is the reason Amazon's hit rate on new products is structurally higher than competitors'.

## Key Frameworks

### 1. The PR-FAQ (Press Release / Frequently Asked Questions)
Before any new initiative is approved, its champions must write:
- **Press Release:** A mock press release dated to the product's launch, written from the customer's perspective. Present-tense, non-technical, explains the benefit and why it matters. Typically one page.
- **FAQ:** Answers to the hard questions — customer questions (what problem does this solve, how does it work) and internal questions (how much will it cost, what dependencies exist, what are the biggest risks).

The discipline: if you cannot write a compelling customer PR, you do not have a clear product vision. The PR-FAQ is the forcing function for clarity before investment.

### 2. Six-Page Narrative Memos (No PowerPoint)
Amazon banned PowerPoint for senior meetings. Every major decision is presented as a structured prose narrative — typically six pages — which attendees read in silence at the start of the meeting.

Why this matters:
- Prose requires coherent, linear thinking; bullets hide gaps in logic
- Reading together levels the information playing field (no one gets a pre-brief advantage)
- The memo is a living artifact that improves every time it's revised

### 3. Bar Raiser Hiring
Every hire must clear a "Bar Raiser" — a senior Amazonian who is not on the hiring team and whose sole job is to maintain the hiring standard. The Bar Raiser has unilateral veto power. The bar: every new hire must raise the average of the team they join on at least one dimension.

Compounding effect over thousands of hires: mean team quality rises continuously rather than decaying toward mediocrity.

### 4. 14 Leadership Principles as Operating System
Amazon's Leadership Principles are not aspirational values — they are functional decision rules used daily. Leaders are evaluated against them in performance reviews, hiring interviews, and promotion decisions. The principles are explicitly prioritized when they conflict.

Key principles relevant to this wiki:
- **Customer Obsession** — Start with the customer, work backwards
- **Ownership** — Leaders act like owners; they don't say "that's not my job"
- **Invent and Simplify** — Seek simplicity; tolerate being misunderstood
- **Are Right, A Lot** — Strong judgment and good instincts
- **Bias for Action** — Speed matters; many decisions are reversible
- **Think Big** — Small thinking is a self-fulfilling prophecy
- **Dive Deep** — Leaders stay connected to the details; never above any job
- **Disagree and Commit** — Raise objections loudly, then commit fully once decided
- **Deliver Results** — Despite setbacks, meet the bar

### 5. Single-Threaded Owner (STO)
Every initiative has one person who owns it completely — their entire focus. No shared ownership. Diffuse ownership → diffuse accountability.

### 6. Two-Pizza Teams
Teams should be sized so two pizzas could feed them (6–10 people). Larger teams create communication overhead that slows execution. Small, empowered, single-threaded teams move faster.

### 7. Type 1 / Type 2 Decisions
- **Type 1:** Irreversible, high-consequence decisions. Require deliberation, escalation, and senior sign-off.
- **Type 2:** Reversible, lower-stakes decisions. Should be made quickly by the person closest to the issue. Escalating Type 2 decisions is an organizational anti-pattern that destroys speed.

### 8. Input Metrics vs. Output Metrics
Amazon obsessively tracks input metrics (the actions teams take that *produce* results) rather than only output metrics (the results themselves). Output metrics lag is a historical measurement; input metrics are what leaders can actually control and act on.

## Notable Quotes
