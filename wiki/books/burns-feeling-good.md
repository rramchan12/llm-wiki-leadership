# Feeling Good: The New Mood Therapy — David D. Burns (1980; revised 1999)

**One-liner:** Your moods are created by your thoughts, not your circumstances — and by learning to identify and correct distorted thinking patterns, you can reliably improve how you feel.

> **Note:** The substantially revised 1999 edition is the version most clinicians reference today. Chapter structure may differ from the 1980 original.

---

## ⚡ Practical Playbook (Start Here)

### The Triple-Column Technique — run this when your mood crashes

| Automatic Thought | Cognitive Distortion | Rational Response |
|---|---|---|
| Write what you're actually thinking | Name the distortion(s) from the list below | Write a more balanced, evidence-based thought |
| "I'll never get this right" | All-or-nothing thinking, fortune telling | "I've struggled with this before but improved. One setback doesn't define the outcome." |

**Quick-start steps:**
1. Notice the mood shift (anxiety, anger, sadness, guilt)
2. Ask: "What thought just went through my mind?"
3. Write it down verbatim — don't edit
4. Identify which distortion(s) apply
5. Write a rational response — not positive thinking, but *accurate* thinking
6. Re-rate your mood

## Core Thesis

Depression and anxiety are driven by **cognitive distortions** — systematic errors in thinking that feel absolutely real but don't stand up to examination. Burns, a student of Aaron Beck, argues that you don't need to fix your life to fix your mood — you need to fix your *thinking about* your life. CBT provides a structured, learnable method to do this, and controlled studies show it works as well as or better than medication for many people.

## Key Frameworks

1. **[[cognitive-distortions]]** — The 10 patterns of twisted thinking that generate negative moods. The diagnostic lens for all CBT work.
2. **Triple-Column Technique** — Automatic thought → distortion → rational response. The core daily practice.
3. **Daily Mood Log** — Extended version: situation → emotions (rated 0–100) → automatic thoughts → distortions → rational responses → re-rated emotions.
4. **The Burns Depression Checklist (BDC)** — Self-assessment tool to track mood across weeks.
5. **Pleasure-Predicting Sheet** — Schedule activities, predict satisfaction (0–100), record actual satisfaction. Counters the "nothing will feel good" distortion.
6. **Cost-Benefit Analysis** — For stubborn beliefs: list the advantages and disadvantages of holding this belief. Forces rational evaluation of irrational commitments.
7. **Anti-Procrastination Sheet** — Break tasks into steps, predict difficulty/satisfaction, record actuals. Exposes the distortion that tasks are harder and less rewarding than they actually are.

## The 10 Cognitive Distortions

| # | Distortion | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **All-or-nothing thinking** | Black/white, no middle ground | "If I'm not perfect, I'm a failure" |
| 2 | **Overgeneralization** | One event → universal rule | "I got rejected, I always get rejected" |
| 3 | **Mental filter** | Dwell on one negative, ignore positive | A great review with one criticism = "I'm terrible" |
| 4 | **Disqualifying the positive** | Dismiss good things as flukes | "They only said that to be nice" |
| 5 | **Jumping to conclusions** | Mind reading or fortune telling | "She thinks I'm incompetent" / "This won't work" |
| 6 | **Magnification/minimization** | Exaggerate bad, shrink good | Catastrophizing a small mistake |
| 7 | **Emotional reasoning** | "I feel it, so it must be true" | "I feel anxious, so this situation is dangerous" |
| 8 | **Should statements** | Rigid rules about self/others | "I should always be productive" |
| 9 | **Labeling** | Attaching a global label to one event | "I'm a loser" instead of "I made a mistake" |
| 10 | **Personalization** | Taking responsibility for things outside your control | "The meeting went badly because of me" |
