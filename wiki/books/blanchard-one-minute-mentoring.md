# One Minute Mentoring — Kenneth Blanchard & Claire Shankman (2017)

**One-liner:** Intentional mentoring partnerships — built on mutual trust, clear goals, and reciprocal learning — are the accelerant of leadership development that most organizations systematically underuse.

## Core Thesis

Most mentoring is casual, informal, and therefore inconsistent. Blanchard and Shankman argue that mentoring, like management, can be structured into a reliable process without losing its human warmth. Both parties benefit: mentors gain fresh perspective and renewed purpose; protégés gain shortcuts born of experience. Reciprocal learning is built into the model.

The book is written as a parable, following Josh — a rising manager — and his mentor Diane, as they navigate a real-world mentoring relationship.

## Key Frameworks

### The MENTOR Acronym [VERIFY: exact letter definitions]

A framework summarizing the mentor's responsibilities:

- **M — Motivate for development:** Help the protégé see their growth edge and inspire action toward it
- **E — Emphasize development of character:** Leadership is about who you are, not just what you know
- **N — Navigate learning opportunities:** Guide the protégé toward experiences that stretch them appropriately
- **T — Take an active role:** Mentoring requires deliberate effort, not just availability
- **O — Open connections to your network:** Introduce the protégé to people, perspectives, and opportunities they couldn't access alone
- **R — Reward risk-taking:** Acknowledge when the protégé pushes their edges — success or failure

### The Five Phases of a Mentoring Partnership

1. **Define purpose and goals** — What does the protégé want to achieve? What are 90-day and 12-month learning goals?
2. **Build the relationship** — Establish trust, communication norms, and psychological safety (see [[psychological-safety]])
3. **Determine the development plan** — Structured learning activities, stretch assignments, regular meetings
4. **Execute the plan** — The protégé does the work; mentor provides reflection, feedback, and challenge
5. **Wrap up and celebrate** — Explicitly close the formal mentoring engagement; articulate what was learned and how the relationship continues

### Mentor and Protégé Responsibilities

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|-----------------|
| **Mentor** | Perspective, challenge, honest feedback, network, time, encouragement |
| **Protégé** | Clear goals, openness to feedback, accountability, preparation, respect for the mentor's time |

Neither party can succeed without the other meeting their responsibilities. A protégé who doesn't prepare for meetings or act on advice wastes the mentor's investment. A mentor who doesn't challenge or is available only in name fails the protégé.

### Reverse Mentoring

One of the book's notable extensions: protégés have things to teach mentors too — especially about technology, new generations, and fresh perspectives. The model explicitly invites mentors to learn from protégés, normalizing a two-way exchange.

### Being Both Mentor and Protégé

Blanchard argues experienced leaders should always maintain both relationships simultaneously: a professional mentor above them (to continue growing) and a protégé below them (to stay current and give back). Neither role is optional once you reach a certain level.
