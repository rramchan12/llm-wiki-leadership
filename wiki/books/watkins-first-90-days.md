# The First 90 Days — Michael D. Watkins (2003, updated 2013)

**One-liner:** How you navigate the first 90 days of a new leadership role determines your long-term success — accelerate your transition with a structured approach to learning, building alliances, and securing early wins.

---

## ⚡ Practical Playbook (Start Here)

### The 90-Day Checklist

**Days 1–30 — Learn and Diagnose**
- [ ] Match your strategy to the **STARS situation** (see below)
- [ ] Conduct a **systematic learning plan** — who knows what you need to know?
- [ ] Schedule **one-on-one conversations** with 10+ key stakeholders using the same 5 questions
- [ ] Identify the **prevailing culture** — how are decisions made, what's rewarded, what's taboo?
- [ ] Negotiate your **mandate and expectations** with your boss — align on 90-day success criteria

**Days 30–60 — Build the Architecture**
- [ ] **Secure early wins** — visible, meaningful results that build credibility and momentum
- [ ] Assess your **team**: who's A-player, who's misaligned, who's a question mark?
- [ ] Begin building **coalitions** — map the political landscape, identify allies and resistors
- [ ] Create your **90-day plan** and share it with your boss for alignment

**Days 60–90 — Drive and Align**
- [ ] Deepen **alliances** — convert skeptics, reinforce supporters
- [ ] Begin making the **harder calls** (restructuring, realignment) with credibility earned from early wins
- [ ] Establish **repeatable processes** for communication, decision-making, and team rhythms
- [ ] Conduct a formal **90-day review** with your boss

## Core Thesis

Transitions are the most critical — and most dangerous — moments in a leader's career. The actions you take during your first 90 days in a new role either create a virtuous cycle of credibility and momentum or a vicious cycle of missteps and lost trust. Most leaders fail in new roles not from lack of competence but from failure to adapt: they apply old playbooks to new contexts, ignore political dynamics, or try to do too much (or too little) too fast. Watkins provides a structured framework to accelerate the learning curve and establish yourself effectively.

## Key Frameworks

1. **[[transition-acceleration]]** — The overarching concept: treat transitions as a distinct discipline requiring specific skills, not just "doing more of what got you promoted."
2. **STARS Model** — Five business situations that require fundamentally different strategies:

| Situation | What It Is | Your Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **S**tart-up | Building something new from scratch | Define vision, recruit, move fast |
| **T**urnaround | Saving a failing business | Diagnose urgently, make tough calls early |
| **A**ccelerated growth | Scaling what's working | Build structure without killing the engine |
| **R**ealignment | Redirecting a drifting-but-not-failing org | Build urgency where none exists |
| **S**ustaining success | Maintaining momentum in a winning org | Don't fix what isn't broken; find the next challenge |

3. **[[early-wins]]** — Securing visible, meaningful victories in the first 30–60 days that build credibility and create positive momentum.
4. **The Five Conversations with Your Boss** — Structured alignment conversations about: (1) the situation, (2) expectations, (3) resources, (4) style, (5) personal development.
5. **Coalition Building** — Mapping influence networks: who are the allies, resistors, and persuadables? Sequence your engagement strategically.
6. **Team Assessment** — Evaluate inherited team members along two dimensions: competence and alignment. Retain/develop/replace framework.
7. **Matching Strategy to Situation** — The STARS diagnostic prevents applying a turnaround playbook to a sustaining-success situation (or vice versa).

## The Political Landscape Map

| Category | Who They Are | Your Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong supporters** | Already aligned with you and your agenda | Reinforce; don't take for granted |
| **Allies** | Generally supportive but not yet committed | Engage early; provide information; invite participation |
| **Fence-sitters** | Waiting to see which way the wind blows | Court carefully; early wins move them |
| **Opponents** | Philosophically disagreeing with your approach | Understand their concerns; find common ground if possible |
| **Blockers** | Actively working against you | Isolate or neutralize; don't invest disproportionate energy |

## Chapter Summaries

| Ch. | Focus | Key Insight |
|-----|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Prepare yourself | The skills that got you here won't be enough. Transition is a distinct challenge. |
| 2 | Accelerate your learning | Systematic learning > random collecting. Have a plan for what you need to learn. |
| 3 | Match strategy to situation | Use STARS. A turnaround ≠ a realignment ≠ a start-up. |
| 4 | Negotiate success | The five conversations with your boss. Align expectations explicitly. |
| 5 | Secure early wins | Visible, quick, meaningful. Build credibility before attempting big changes. |
| 6 | Achieve alignment | Align strategy, structure, systems, skills, and culture (the STARS-adjacent organizational model). |
| 7 | Build your team | Assess, restructure if needed. The inherited team may not be the right team. |
| 8 | Create coalitions | Map the political landscape. Influence ≠ authority. Build alliances before you need them. |
| 9 | Keep your balance | Transition stress is real. Build support systems; don't become your role. |
| 10 | Accelerate everyone | Use these principles to help your whole team through their transitions. |

## Notable Quotes

- "Transitions are periods of opportunity, a chance to start afresh and to make needed changes. But they are also periods of acute vulnerability."
- "Leaders in new roles are expected to diagnose the situation, build coalitions, and begin driving change — all while learning the job."
- "Early wins build personal credibility and create momentum."
- "The most dangerous transition trap is applying your old playbook to a new situation."
