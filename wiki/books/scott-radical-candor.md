# Radical Candor — Kim Scott (2017)

**One-liner:** Great bosses care personally AND challenge directly — most fail at one or both, producing Ruinous Empathy, Obnoxious Aggression, or Manipulative Insincerity.

## Core Thesis

The single most important thing a boss can do is give and receive honest, caring feedback. This requires simultaneously caring personally about employees as human beings AND challenging them directly when their work isn't good enough. Most managers default to Ruinous Empathy (caring without challenging) — it feels kind but causes the most harm. Radical Candor is not a personality type but a practice calibrated to each relationship.

## Key Frameworks

### The Radical Candor 2×2

|  | Challenge Directly ↑ | Challenge Directly ↓ |
|--|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Care Personally ↑** | **Radical Candor** ✓ | **Ruinous Empathy** |
| **Care Personally ↓** | **Obnoxious Aggression** | **Manipulative Insincerity** |

- **Radical Candor:** Care + Challenge. The goal.
- **Ruinous Empathy:** Care without Challenge. The most common failure mode. Feels kind, actually harmful.
- **Obnoxious Aggression:** Challenge without Care. At least honest, but destroys trust.
- **Manipulative Insincerity:** Neither. Passive-aggression and political games.

### Feedback Order of Operations
1. Solicit feedback before giving it ("What could I do differently?")
2. Give specific, sincere praise first (behavior + impact, not "Good job")
3. Give criticism in private (behavior + impact + expectation)
4. Calibrate to the individual (warmth vs. directness needs)

### Rockstars vs. Superstars
- **Rockstars:** Stable excellent performers who love their current role → recognize, protect stability
- **Superstars:** Growth-trajectory performers who need new challenges → provide stretch opportunities
- Mismanaging either causes attrition

### GSD (Get Stuff Done) Wheel
Listen → Clarify → Debate → Decide → Persuade → Execute → Learn → repeat. Prevents both autocracy and consensus-paralysis.
