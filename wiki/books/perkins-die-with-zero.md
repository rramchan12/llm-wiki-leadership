# Die With Zero — Bill Perkins (2020)

> **LLM Confidence: MEDIUM** — Core arguments and frameworks are well-known; some specific rules, thresholds, and chapter details marked `[VERIFY]`.

**One-liner:** Your goal should not be to maximize wealth at death but to maximize life experiences — because experiences compound as memories, and your ability to have them declines with age whether or not you have money.

## Core Thesis

Most people are optimizing the wrong thing: they accumulate money to spend "someday," but that someday keeps retreating. Meanwhile, their health, energy, social network, and appetite for experience decline with age — so money saved for 75-year-old you is far less valuable than experiences had at 35 or 45. Perkins argues for **zero-based life accounting**: spend your life energy (time × health × money) on experiences that generate the highest memory returns at the right life stages. Die with (close to) zero unspent potential.

## Key Frameworks

### 1. [[memory-dividend]]
Experiences are not consumed at the moment of purchase — they pay dividends for the rest of your life through the memories they generate. A great trip at 30 will be remembered at 40, 50, 60, 70. The **memory dividend compounds over time**: the earlier in life you have rich experiences, the more dividends they pay. This inverts the conventional logic of deferral ("I'll travel when I retire").

Key formula concept: `Experience value ≠ cost at purchase; Experience value = present enjoyment + lifetime memory dividends`

### 2. [[time-buckets]]
Divide your anticipated life into roughly 5–10 year buckets. For each bucket, ask: **what experiences are uniquely possible, or much better, in this life stage?** Then deliberately schedule and fund those experiences — before the window closes.

Examples:
- 20s–30s: backpacking, physically demanding adventure, building relationships
- 40s–50s: peak career engagement, experiential travel with growing children
- 60s–70s: experiences requiring moderate health, gift-giving while alive to see the impact
- 70s+: experiences possible with reduced mobility; more reflective pleasures

The principle: **don't defer bucket 35 experiences to age 65**. Physical capability, social context, and appetite for certain types of experience are irreversible.

### 3. Personal Interest/Health Curve `[VERIFY specific details]`
Perkins describes a curve where your ability to engage in experiences peaks in mid-life and declines thereafter. Overlaying your wealth curve (which typically rises later) reveals a **mismatch**: most people have the most money when they have the least capacity to fully use it. The fix is to shift spending left — earlier, when the health curve is favorable.

### 4. Give While Living (vs. Estate Planning)
Perkins challenges the default of leaving large inheritances at death. His argument: **giving money to children or causes at 35 or 45 has far more impact than giving it at 85** — both because the recipients are younger and can use it better, and because you get to see the impact. The "ideal" estate at death: close to zero. `[VERIFY specific age thresholds]`

### 5. Maximize Experience Points, Not Net Worth
Reframe life success from wealth accumulation to "experience points" — a deliberate metaphor from gaming. Each meaningful experience adds to a score that compounds. The goal: maximize the total experiential richness of a life, within real constraints of time and health. Money is an instrument, not a destination.

### 6. The 45/70 Rule `[VERIFY]`
Perkins suggests beginning to draw down assets more aggressively around age 45–50, and aiming to have substantially less by 70. The goal is to avoid the trap of wealthy but low-vitality late life. `[VERIFY exact thresholds and methodology]`
