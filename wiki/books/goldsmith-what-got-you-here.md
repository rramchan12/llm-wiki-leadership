# What Got You Here Won't Get You There — Marshall Goldsmith (2007)

**One-liner:** The interpersonal behaviors that drove your past success become the very habits that cap your future growth — identifying and shedding them is the real work of senior leadership.

## Core Thesis

Success creates a paradox: the drive, confidence, and competitive habits that earned you your position start working against you at higher levels. This is the "success delusion" — believing your behavior is fine because you've succeeded so far. Leadership at the top is overwhelmingly relational, not technical. The margin between good and great leaders is almost never skill; it is almost always behavior.

Goldsmith draws on decades of executive coaching to identify **20 habits** that hold otherwise excellent leaders back — and a structured process (feedforward, apology, follow-up) for changing them.

## Key Frameworks

### The 20 Habits That Hold Leaders Back

1. **Winning too much** — the need to win at everything, even when it doesn't matter
2. **Adding too much value** — always having to improve every idea
3. **Passing judgment** — rating others and imposing your standards
4. **Making destructive comments** — sarcasm, cutting humor that "tells the truth"
5. **Starting with "No," "But," or "However"** — negating before engaging
6. **Telling the world how smart we are** — need to prove you're the smartest
7. **Speaking when angry** — emotional volatility as a management tool
8. **Negativity** — "Let me explain why that won't work"
9. **Withholding information** — failing to share credit or context
10. **Failing to give proper recognition**
11. **Claiming credit we don't deserve**
12. **Making excuses**
13. **Clinging to the past**
14. **Playing favorites**
15. **Refusing to express regret**
16. **Not listening**
17. **Failing to express gratitude**
18. **Punishing the messenger**
19. **Passing the buck**
20. **An excessive need to be "me"** — using personality as an excuse

### The Change Process

1. **Get feedback** — 360 from peers, reports, and boss; hear the pattern
2. **Apologize** — brief, unqualified. No "but." Earns the right to change
3. **Advertise** — tell people you are working on a specific behavior
4. **Listen** — when they give you input, say "thank you"; do not justify
5. **Follow up** — at regular intervals, ask "Am I improving on X?"
6. **Feed-forward** — instead of asking for feedback on what you did wrong, ask for **two suggestions for the future** (see [[feedforward]])

### The Feedforward Tool

Feedback looks backward; feedforward looks forward. Ask: *"Can you give me two suggestions to help me become a better [X] in the future?"* Listen and say "thank you." No debate, no defense. Goldsmith finds this more actionable, less threatening, and dramatically more efficient than traditional 360 feedback.

### The Success Delusion

When we succeed, we naturally attribute success to our own behavior. This causal attribution error hardens over time: "I'm successful. My behavior causes success. Therefore my behavior must be fine." This loop is nearly invulnerable to self-correction — only structured external feedback breaks it.
