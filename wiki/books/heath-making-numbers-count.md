# Making Numbers Count — Chip Heath & Karla Starr (2022)

**One-liner:** Numbers only persuade when they are translated into concrete, human-scale experiences — the art of numeracy translation is the highest leverage communication skill for data-driven leaders.

## Core Thesis

Most people present numbers the way they receive them — as abstract quantities. But the human brain evolved to understand the world through sensory experience, story, and proportion, not through statistics. A number that cannot be felt cannot be acted upon.

Heath and Starr argue that the gap between data and decision is bridged not by adding more data, but by translating data into experiences the audience can feel. This is numeracy translation: the deliberate conversion of abstract quantities into concrete, vivid, human-scale comparisons that land in the gut, not just the head.

The book is the numerical complement to Heath's earlier *Made to Stick* — where SUCCESs provides general sticky-communication principles, this book goes deep on the single most common failure point for leaders: numbers.

## Key Frameworks

### 1. The Concrete Comparison
Replace raw numbers with physical, spatial, or temporal analogies the audience already understands.
- "1 billion seconds is 31.7 years." [VERIFY specific example]
- Scale to the audience's familiar world: bodies, sports fields, commutes, grocery bills.

### 2. Conversion to Human Scale
Reduce large or small numbers to a per-person, per-day, or single-unit frame.
- National debt → dollars per taxpayer per year
- Corporate emissions → per product unit, per mile driven

### 3. Emotional Concreteness (The One Person Rule)
Statistics numb; individuals move. When data involves people, zoom in to one. [VERIFY: attributed to Mother Teresa quote or similar]
- Report on "Maria, a 34-year-old nurse in Cleveland" not "3.2 million uninsured workers."

### 4. Proportion and Comparison
Humans understand relative magnitude far better than absolute numbers.
- "This tumor is the size of a golf ball" is more actionable than "3.8 centimeters."
- Always anchor the comparison to something deeply familiar.

### 5. Experiential Translation [VERIFY chapter placement]
Turn numbers into mini-scenarios the audience can mentally simulate.
- "If your company's overhead were a 100-calorie snack, benefits would be 74 of those calories." [VERIFY]

### 6. Pattern Interruption
Open with a counterintuitive number to create a curiosity gap (the Unexpected principle from *Made to Stick*) — then satisfy it with a concrete translation.
