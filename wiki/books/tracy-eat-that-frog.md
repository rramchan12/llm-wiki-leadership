# Eat That Frog! — Brian Tracy (2001)

**One-liner:** Start every day by doing your most important task first — and build 21 practical habits that systematically destroy procrastination before it destroys your results.

## Core Thesis

Procrastination is universal. Everyone procrastinates on something. The goal is not to eliminate procrastination but to **procrastinate on low-value activities** while doing high-value work first. Tracy's organizing metaphor, attributed to Mark Twain: "If it's your job to eat a live frog, it's best to do it first thing in the morning. And if it's your job to eat two frogs, eat the biggest one first."

The "frog" is your most important, most impactful, highest-leverage task — and almost certainly the one you're most inclined to avoid. Everything else in the book is a system for identifying the frog, eating it immediately, and building the habits that make this your default.

## Key Frameworks

### The ABCDE Priority Method

The central tool for daily task prioritization:

| Category | Definition | Action |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **A** | Critical. Must do. Serious consequences for not doing. The Frog. | Do first — and do A1 before A2 |
| **B** | Should do. Mild consequences if not done. | Do after all A tasks are complete |
| **C** | Nice to do. No real consequences either way. | Do only after all A and B tasks complete |
| **D** | Delegate. Someone else can do this. | Pass it down |
| **E** | Eliminate. Not worth doing at all. | Remove from list |

The iron rule: **Never do a B task while an A task remains undone.** The B task feels urgent and easier — that's precisely why it seduces you away from the A.

### The 80/20 Rule (Pareto Applied to Tasks)

20% of tasks produce 80% of results. The productive person identifies that 20% and focuses there relentlessly. Warning: the 80% of low-value tasks are not without work — they feel busy, important, urgent — but they don't move the needle. Confusing activity with achievement is the central trap.

### Key Result Areas (KRAs)

Every job has 5–7 Key Result Areas — the outputs you are specifically paid to deliver. Everything else is support activity:
1. Identify your KRAs (what must you deliver excellently to be fully effective?)
2. Rank them by impact
3. Identify your weakest KRA — it sets your performance ceiling
4. Build daily work around improving in the highest-impact KRA

This is Tracy's version of the Theory of Constraints applied to personal performance (cf. [[theory-of-constraints]]).

### The Three Most Important Tasks (Law of Three)

Each morning, ask: **If I could only do three things today, what three tasks would contribute the most value?** These three tasks become the non-negotiable core of the day. Everything else is secondary.

### Single-Handling

Once you start your most important task, **don't stop until it's 100% complete**. Every interruption forces restart overhead — context loading, re-engagement, momentum rebuilding. Starting and stopping multiplies the effective time cost. Single-handling = eat the frog without putting it down.

### Creative Procrastination

Deliberately choose to procrastinate on your **C and D tasks** to protect time for A tasks. This is active management of your task list, not passive drift. The question is not "am I procrastinating?" — you always are on something. The question is "am I procrastinating on the right things?"

### Prepare Thoroughly Before You Begin

The single most powerful way to beat start-resistance is to prepare everything in advance:
- Clear the workspace
- Gather materials
- Have the document open or the tools laid out

Preparation eliminates the friction of starting. The frog is hardest in the abstract.

### Upgrade Your Skills Continuously

Competence creates momentum. Tracy emphasizes that procrastination often stems from **skill anxiety** — we avoid tasks we don't feel confident completing. The remedy: invest in the skills your most important tasks require. Every hour of learning compounds.

## The 21 Techniques (Chapter Overview)

Tracy organizes 21 practical techniques across three broad phases:
1. **Clarity** (Chapters 1–5): Set the table; plan daily; apply 80/20; consider consequences; practice creative procrastination
2. **Focus** (Chapters 6–14): ABCDE; KRAs; Law of Three; prepare before starting; take it one step at a time; upgrade skills; identify key constraints; put pressure on yourself; maximize strengths
3. **Execution** (Chapters 15–21): Technology as servant; focus on key tasks; slice and dice tasks; create large time blocks; develop sense of urgency; single-handle every task

## Notable Quotes

> "Eat a live frog first thing in the morning and nothing worse will happen to you the rest of the day." (attributed to Mark Twain)

> "The more you practice, the easier it becomes. The easier it becomes, the more you do it. The more you do it, the better you get."

> "Every minute you spend in planning saves 10 minutes in execution."

> "The ability to concentrate single-mindedly on your most important task, to do it well and to finish it completely, is the key to great success."
