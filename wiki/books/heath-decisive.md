# Decisive — Chip Heath & Dan Heath (2013)

**One-liner:** Counter the four predictable villains of bad decision-making with the WRAP framework — a repeatable process that widens options, stress-tests assumptions, neutralizes emotion, and plans for error.

---

## ⚡ Practical Playbook (Start Here)

### The WRAP Checklist — run this on any significant decision

**W — Widen your options**
- [ ] Am I seeing this as a binary choice? Apply the **vanishing options test**: "If none of these options existed, what else could I do?"
- [ ] Can I **multi-track** — pursue two options in parallel rather than sequentially?
- [ ] Has someone already solved this? **Find a bright spot** (internal) or an **analogous situation** (external).
- [ ] Can I **ladder up** (what's the deeper goal?) or **ladder down** (what's the smallest possible experiment)?

**R — Reality-test assumptions**
- [ ] What would I need to believe for each option to be the right one?
- [ ] What would a **devil's advocate** or **skeptic** say? Have I actively sought that view?
- [ ] Can I **ooch** — run a cheap experiment before full commitment?
- [ ] What do **base rates** say? What do people who have done this before actually report?

**A — Attain distance before deciding**
- [ ] Run the **10/10/10**: How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
- [ ] Ask: "What would I advise my **best friend** to do in this situation?"
- [ ] Have I clarified my **core priorities** independently of this decision? Am I honoring them?
- [ ] Is there a **status quo bias** holding me back? Run the "new CEO through the window" test.

**P — Prepare to be wrong**
- [ ] Run a **premortem**: "It's one year later — this failed. What went wrong?"
- [ ] Run a **pre-parade**: "It's one year later — this worked brilliantly. What made it succeed?"
- [ ] Have I **bookended** the future (disaster + breakthrough scenarios)?
- [ ] Have I set a **tripwire** — a clear condition that will trigger a decision review?

## Core Thesis

Most bad decisions aren't caused by stupidity — they're caused by predictable cognitive biases that operate below awareness. The Heaths call these the **Four Villains**: narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, and overconfidence. The WRAP framework is a four-step process that directly counters each villain, making better decisions achievable through process, not just intelligence.

---

## Key Frameworks

1. **[[four-villains-of-decision-making]]** — The four biases that reliably derail decisions: narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, overconfidence.
2. **[[wrap-framework]]** — The four-step decision process: Widen options, Reality-test assumptions, Attain distance, Prepare to be wrong.
3. **[[premortem]]** — "It's one year from now. We failed. What happened?" A structured way to surface hidden risks before committing.
4. **[[tripwires]]** — Pre-set conditions that automatically trigger a decision review, preventing sunk-cost escalation.
5. **Ooching** — Running small experiments before full commitment. Tests assumptions cheaply, avoids over-planning.
6. **10/10/10 Analysis** — How will you feel about this in 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years? A temporal ladder to escape short-term emotion.
7. **Vanishing Options Test** — "What if none of these options were available?" Forces creative expansion of the option space.
8. **Core Priorities Clarification** — Pre-deciding what matters before emotions cloud the call.

## Key Examples

- **Quaker / Snapple** — $1.7B acquisition destroyed. Classic confirmation bias: bullish narrative blocked disconfirming signals.
- **Intel's pivot** — Andy Grove's "new CEO through the window" test liberated them from the sunk-cost of memory chips to microprocessors.
- **The Intertek engineer** — Ooched her way into a new career. Small experiments, not a leap of faith.
- **The nurse and the newborn** — Expert intuition overrode protocol correctly; underscores that emotion ≠ always wrong, but process catches what intuition misses.
