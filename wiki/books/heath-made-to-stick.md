# Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath (2007)

**One-liner:** Ideas stick when they are Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and told as Stories — the SUCCESs framework defeats the Curse of Knowledge.

## Core Thesis

The "Curse of Knowledge" — once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it — is the root of most communication failure. Leaders default to abstraction and jargon because that's how they think. The six SUCCESs principles provide a systematic toolkit to make any idea memorable and actionable, from corporate strategy to team feedback.

## Key Frameworks

### SUCCESs Checklist

| Principle | What It Does | Sticky Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Simple** | Find the core; strip to the essential idea | "THE low-fare airline" (Southwest) |
| **Unexpected** | Break patterns (surprise) → hold attention (curiosity) | Nordstrom service legends |
| **Concrete** | Make abstract tangible; sensory language | Aesop's fables beat management platitudes |
| **Credible** | External authority + internal vivid detail + testability | The Sinatra Test: "If I can make it there…" |
| **Emotional** | People care about people, not numbers; appeal to identity | Mother Teresa: "If I look at the one…" |
| **Stories** | Mental flight simulators; three plots: Challenge, Connection, Creativity | Jared from Subway |

### The Curse of Knowledge

The fundamental barrier: experts can't un-know what they know. They communicate in abstractions that resonate with peers but not with their audience. Every SUCCESs principle is designed to puncture this bubble.

### Commander's Intent

The military's answer to Simple: distill the plan to the one thing that must survive when everything else falls apart. The leadership equivalent: "If my team remembers only one thing from this meeting, what must it be?"

## Chapter Summaries

- **Ch 1 (Simple):** Commander's Intent, Southwest Airlines, inverted pyramid. Core over comprehensive.
- **Ch 2 (Unexpected):** Curiosity gaps (Loewenstein), Nordstrom legends, the Roone Arledge approach.
- **Ch 3 (Concrete):** Asian velvet, HP's "names, dates, and places," white things vs. white things in a fridge.
- **Ch 4 (Credible):** Sinatra Test, anti-authority endorsements, testable credentials.
- **Ch 5 (Emotional):** Mother Teresa, semantic stretch, identity-based appeals.
- **Ch 6 (Stories):** Jared's Challenge plot, the three plot templates, stories as simulation.

## Notable Quotes

- "The Curse of Knowledge: once we know something, we find it hard to imagine what it was like not to know it."
- "Concrete is memorable. Abstract is forgettable."
- "Stories are flight simulators for the brain."
- "If I look at the mass, I will never act. If I look at the one, I will."
