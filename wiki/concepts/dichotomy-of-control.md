# Dichotomy of Control

**Definition:** A Stoic mental model that separates what is up to us (judgment, intention, effort, behavior) from what is not (other people, market outcomes, past events, external shocks).

## Why It Matters

Leaders under pressure often waste energy trying to control the uncontrollable. This model restores agency: focus where action is possible, then respond to outcomes without denial or rumination. The result is better decisions, calmer teams, and more consistent execution.

## Control Filter

1. What part of this situation is directly under my control right now?
2. What part is influenceable but not controllable?
3. What part is outside both control and influence?
4. What is the next virtuous action anyway?

## Covey's Circle of Influence

Covey operationalizes this Stoic insight as two concentric circles:

```
     ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
     │          Circle of Concern          │
     │   (everything that affects you)     │
     │                                     │
     │   ┌────────────────────────────┐    │
     │   │    Circle of Influence     │    │
     │   │  (what you can affect)     │    │
     │   └────────────────────────────┘    │
     └─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

| Focus | Effect on Circle |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Concern (outside influence)** | Circle of Influence **shrinks** — energy wasted on helplessness |
| **Influence (inside)** | Circle of Influence **expands** — results build credibility and create more influence |

**Reactive vs. Proactive Language** — a diagnostic for which circle governs thinking:

| Reactive (Concern focus) | Proactive (Influence focus) |
|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| "There's nothing I can do" | "Let's look at our options" |
| "That's just the way I am" | "I can choose a different approach" |
| "They make me so angry" | "I control how I respond" |
| "If only my boss were different" | "Given my boss, what can I do?" |
| "I have to" | "I choose to" |

Covey's Habit 1 (Be Proactive) is built on Frankl's claim: between any stimulus and your response, there is a gap — and in that gap lies your freedom and responsibility.

## How Books Treat This

| Book | Stance | Key Insight |
|------|--------|-------------|
| [[stoic-leader]] | Defines | Focus on standards of response, not guarantees of external success. |
| [[covey-seven-habits]] | Operationalizes | Habit 1: Be Proactive. Circle of Influence focus is the first private victory. |
| [[heath-decisive]] | Complements | WRAP improves decision process; control filter clarifies where to apply that process. |
| [[harris-happiness-trap]] | Parallels | Acceptance + committed action mirrors Stoic focus on action despite discomfort. ACT's committed action is always within the Circle of Influence. |
| [[goldsmith-what-got-you-here]] | Applied | Feedforward is entirely within the Circle of Influence: you cannot change past behavior, but you can change future behavior. |
| [[seneca-letters-from-a-stoic]] | Primary source | *"Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est"* — All things are foreign; time alone is ours. Time is the canonical Stoic example of what is genuinely under our stewardship. |
| [[aurelius-meditations]] | Primary source | "You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this and you will find strength." Also: the inner citadel — no external event can breach the faculty of judgment without your consent. |
| [[frankl-mans-search-for-meaning]] | Most radical version | Frankl's "last human freedom" — the freedom to choose your response to unavoidable circumstances — is the dichotomy of control taken to its logical extreme: available even in a concentration camp. |
| [[jorgenson-naval-almanack]] | Modernizes | "Play long-term games" requires releasing attachment to short-term outcomes — a secular, practical application of the same mental model. |
