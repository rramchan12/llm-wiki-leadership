# Leadership Principles as Operating System

**Definition:** The practice of codifying an organization's or leader's core beliefs into explicit, prioritized, operational principles that function as decision-making rules — not aspirational posters — used daily in hiring, performance reviews, conflict resolution, and strategic choices.

> **Source: LLM knowledge | Confidence: HIGH**

---

## Why It Matters

Most organizations have values. Almost none use them as decision-making tools. The result is "culture by default" — implicit norms that favor whoever is loudest or most senior rather than what actually serves the mission. Explicit leadership principles, enforced through mechanisms and modeled by leaders, create a self-consistent operating system that scales: every employee at every level makes better-aligned decisions without needing to escalate every judgment call.

Three books in this wiki represent three distinct implementations of this idea: [[bryar-working-backwards]] (Amazon's 14 Leadership Principles + mechanisms), [[willink-extreme-ownership]] (SEAL-derived ownership and decentralization principles), and [[dalio-principles]] (Bridgewater's algorithmic, believability-weighted decision rules).

---

## The Core Pattern

Across all three implementations, explicit principles as an OS share five features:

1. **Written and specific, not vague.** "Be bold" is not a principle. "Disagree and commit: raise your objections loudly, then support the decision fully once made" is one.
2. **Prioritized when they conflict.** Every principle system faces trade-offs. The OS is explicit about which principle wins.
3. **Used in evaluation.** Performance reviews, promotions, hiring decisions reference the principles directly. If you can't be evaluated on it, it's not operative.
4. **Modeled by leaders first.** Principles only spread downward if the senior leadership team lives them visibly, especially when it's costly to do so.
5. **Updated deliberately, not continuously.** The principles evolve, but slowly and explicitly — not by drift.

## Three Implementations

### Amazon — 14 Leadership Principles (Bryar & Carr)
- Explicit set of principles, down-selected from thousands of proposals over years
- Used verbatim in every hiring interview (behavioral questions map to specific principles)
- Performance reviews score leaders against principles
- Mechanisms (PR-FAQ, Bar Raiser, two-pizza teams) *enforce* principles rather than relying on exhortation
- See [[working-backwards-pr-faq]] and [[bryar-working-backwards]]

### Bridgewater — Principles (Dalio)
- Ray Dalio wrote down every decision rule he used, eventually producing 210+ principles
- "Believability-weighted decision-making": not one-person-one-vote; votes are weighted by domain track record
- Radical transparency: all meetings recorded, all reasoning documented
- Treats the organization as a machine: diagnose root causes, adjust systematically
- See [[dalio-principles]], [[radical-transparency-and-algorithms]]

### SEAL Teams — Extreme Ownership (Willink & Babin)
- Fewer principles, but non-negotiable: leader owns all outcomes; no excuses
- Decentralized Command: authority pushed to lowest competent level within commander's intent
- Cover and Move, Priority and Execute, Keep It Simple
- Principles are expressed through after-action reviews, where accountability is structural not personal
- See [[extreme-ownership]], [[decentralized-command]], [[willink-extreme-ownership]]

## What Makes the Difference (Mechanisms vs. Exhortation)

The critical insight from Bryar/Carr: **most organizations already have principles; what they lack is mechanisms that enforce them.** Culture is not what leaders say — it's what the system rewards and punishes. Mechanisms that make principles *costly to violate* and *easy to follow* are what separate aspirational wall-posters from functional operating systems.

| Principle | Mechanism (Amazon example) |
|---|---|
| Hire the best | Bar Raiser — can't be overridden |
| Think long-term | Single-threaded owner; no shared accountability |
| Customer obsession | PR-FAQ must pass the customer-language test |
| Disagree and commit | Meeting formats require public dissent, then commit on record |
