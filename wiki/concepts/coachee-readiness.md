# Coachee Readiness (Situational Leadership)

**Definition:** The principle that the appropriate leadership and coaching style must match the individual's current development level for a specific task — not their general seniority, not the leader's preferred style, and not one fixed approach for all situations.

## Why It Matters

The most common delegation failure is applying the same management style regardless of the person's readiness. Over-directing a competent expert insults them and kills motivation. Under-directing a novice abandons them and guarantees failure. Blanchard's Situational Leadership model provides the diagnostic: assess the person's development level *for this particular task*, then match your style accordingly.

This is not a personality assessment. A person can be D4 (high skill, high commitment) on one task and D1 (low skill, high enthusiasm) on another — simultaneously. The unit of analysis is always **person × task**.

## The Four Development Levels

| Level | Skill | Commitment | What They Need |
|-------|-------|-----------|----------------|
| **D1 — Enthusiastic Beginner** | Low | High (excited, eager) | Direction: clear goals, step-by-step instruction, close monitoring |
| **D2 — Disillusioned Learner** | Some | Low (discouraged, overwhelmed) | Coaching: continued direction PLUS encouragement and support |
| **D3 — Capable but Cautious** | High | Variable (competent but hesitant) | Support: less direction, more encouragement and collaborative problem-solving |
| **D4 — Self-Reliant Achiever** | High | High (confident, committed) | Delegation: hand off the task with periodic check-ins; trust their judgment |

### The D2 Trap

D2 is the most dangerous stage and the one most leaders mishandle. The person has learned enough to realize how hard the task is, but not enough to feel competent. Enthusiasm drops. At this point:
- **Over-directing** (treating them as D1) signals you don't trust their growing skill
- **Under-directing** (treating them as D3/D4) leaves them unsupported when they need both guidance and encouragement
- **Giving up** on them reads as abandonment

The coaching style for D2 requires the highest investment: continued structure + emotional support + patience.

## Matching Leadership Style to Development Level

| Development Level | Leadership Style | What the Leader Does |
|------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| D1 (Enthusiastic Beginner) | **Directing** — High task, low relationship | Define the goal clearly; explain the steps; set short-cycle check-ins; don't overwhelm with autonomy |
| D2 (Disillusioned Learner) | **Coaching** — High task, high relationship | Continue providing structure AND explain *why*; ask how they're feeling; acknowledge the difficulty; praise progress |
| D3 (Capable but Cautious) | **Supporting** — Low task, high relationship | Let them drive the approach; be available for questions; ask "What do you think?" more than "Here's what to do"; build their confidence through recognition |
| D4 (Self-Reliant Achiever) | **Delegating** — Low task, low relationship | Define the outcome and constraints; let them choose the method; check in periodically; recognize results |

## The Diagnostic Questions

Before any delegation or coaching interaction, the leader should ask:

1. **What is the specific task?** (Not "How good is this person generally?" — readiness is task-specific)
2. **What is their skill level for this task?** (Have they done it before? How recently? How well?)
3. **What is their current motivation/confidence for this task?** (Are they eager, anxious, bored, overwhelmed?)
4. **What style am I defaulting to?** (Most leaders have a habitual style that they apply regardless of context — this is the blind spot)

## Covey's Stewardship Delegation Spectrum

Covey's Habit 3 adds a complementary model. He distinguishes:

| Type | What Happens | When It Works |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Gofer delegation** | "Do this, then this, then this" — leader specifies methods | Only appropriate for D1; kills ownership everywhere else |
| **Stewardship delegation** | Leader specifies desired results, guidelines, resources, accountability, and consequences — person chooses methods | Appropriate for D2–D4; builds capability and ownership |

The progression from gofer to stewardship delegation is the developmental arc. A leader's job is to move people *up* the development levels, not to keep them at D1 forever.

## Connection to Radical Candor

Scott's Rockstars vs. Superstars distinction adds another readiness dimension:

| Type | What They Want | How to Coach |
|------|---------------|-------------|
| **Rockstars** (stable growth) | Recognition, stability, deepening mastery in current role | Support and recognize — don't push toward promotion they don't want |
| **Superstars** (steep growth) | New challenges, stretch assignments, increasing responsibility | Challenge and accelerate — provide the next developmental opportunity |

Misreading a Rockstar as a Superstar (or vice versa) is a readiness failure: you're coaching the wrong person to the wrong goal.

## The Mentoring Layer

[[mentoring-partnership]] extends readiness beyond task-level coaching to career-level development. The protégé's readiness determines the mentoring approach:

- **Early-career protégé:** Needs more structure (define goals, navigate learning opportunities, open network)
- **Mid-career protégé:** Needs more challenge (honest feedback on blind spots, stretch beyond comfort zone)
- **Senior protégé:** Needs perspective (external viewpoints, reverse mentoring, philosophical challenge)
