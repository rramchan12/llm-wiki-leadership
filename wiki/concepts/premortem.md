# Premortem

**Definition:** A structured mental simulation in which you imagine that a decision has already been made and has failed — then work backwards to identify what caused the failure. Developed by psychologist Gary Klein; popularized broadly by the Heaths and Kahneman.

---

## Why It Matters

The standard planning process is optimistic by default: teams build the best case, then add a "risks" section at the end. But the premortem flips the frame. By *assuming failure has already occurred*, it:
1. Liberates people to voice doubts they'd otherwise suppress (social pressure to stay positive is removed)
2. Activates **prospective hindsight** — the brain is better at explaining things that have "already happened"
3. Surfaces specific failure modes, not just vague "risks"

Research by Klein shows that premortems increase the ability to identify reasons for future outcomes by ~30%.

---

## How to Run a Premortem

1. **Set the scenario**: "It is one year from now. We made this decision / launched this project. It has failed — significantly, perhaps spectacularly."
2. **Give everyone 2–3 minutes to write** (individually and silently) the causes of the failure. Writing before sharing prevents anchoring on the first idea mentioned.
3. **Round-robin the group** — each person shares one cause at a time. Capture everything.
4. **Identify the most plausible and highest-impact failure modes.** These become risk-mitigation priorities or tripwires.

**Optional companion: Pre-parade** — run the same exercise assuming success. "It's one year from now. This worked brilliantly. What made it succeed?" Surfaces hidden opportunities and ensures you're not so focused on risk-avoidance that you miss the upside conditions.

---

## When to Use It

- Before committing to a major strategic decision
- Before a product launch or large investment
- Before hiring for a critical role
- Before a significant personal/life decision (business, relationship, move)
- When group dynamics are suppressing dissent

---

## Common Mistakes

- Running it as a group discussion (not individual written → then group) — kills psychological safety
- Treating it as a formality ("we did the premortem, now let's proceed") without actually adjusting the plan
- Focusing only on external risks, ignoring execution and people risks
