# Emotional Bank Account

**Definition:** Covey's metaphor for the trust reserve in a relationship. Behaviors that build trust are deposits; behaviors that erode trust are withdrawals. The account balance determines whether the relationship can carry conflict, honesty, and challenge — or whether friction immediately escalates.

## Why It Matters

Trust is not binary — it exists on a continuum, and it is changed by every interaction. A single broken promise can overdraw an account that took years to build. Conversely, sustained deposits — integrity, genuine attention, honoring commitments — create a reserve that allows misunderstandings to be absorbed without damage.

The practical leadership implication: before you can have a crucial conversation, deliver honest feedback, or drive through a contentious decision, you need an account balance that can absorb the friction. Trying to make a large withdrawal from an empty account produces defensiveness, resistance, or collapse.

## Six Major Deposits

| Deposit | What It Looks Like |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Understanding the individual** | Listening to understand what matters to them — not projecting your priorities onto them |
| **Attending to little things** | Small acts of kindness, remembered details, showing up |
| **Keeping commitments** | Doing what you said, when you said — or proactively communicating if you can't |
| **Clarifying expectations** | Not assuming shared understanding; making the implicit explicit |
| **Showing personal integrity** | Treating the absent person as you would if they were present; being consistent |
| **Apologizing sincerely when you make withdrawals** | "I was wrong; I'm sorry" — no hedging |

## Six Major Withdrawals

| Withdrawal | What It Looks Like |
|------------|-------------------|
| Unkindness, disrespect | Dismissive tone, contempt, sarcasm |
| Breaking a commitment | And not acknowledging it |
| False expectations | Setting up promises you can't keep |
| Arrogance / know-it-all behavior | Not listening; dismissing |
| Disloyalty to absent parties | Talking about people behind their backs |
| Non-apology | "I'm sorry you felt that way" |

## High-Balance vs. Low-Balance Relationships

| High-Balance Account | Low-Balance Account |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Conflict is navigable | Every conflict escalates |
| Honest feedback is received | Honest feedback is defended against |
| Ambiguity is resolved easily | Ambiguity defaults to distrust |
| The relationship can carry load | The relationship is brittle |
