# Transition Acceleration

**Definition:** The deliberate practice of treating leadership transitions as a distinct discipline requiring specific strategies — rather than just "doing more of what got you promoted." The goal is to reach the break-even point (where you're contributing more than you're consuming) as fast as possible.

## Why It Matters

Leaders are most vulnerable during transitions. The skills and relationships that made you successful in your previous role don't automatically transfer. Without a deliberate acceleration strategy, new leaders either (a) apply their old playbook to a fundamentally different situation, (b) try to learn everything at once, or (c) make changes too fast without building credibility. Each of these failure modes is predictable and preventable.

## The Break-Even Point

```
Value contributed
      ↑
      │         ╱──── transition done well
      │        ╱
      │       ╱
      │──────╱────── break-even line
      │     ╱
      │    ╱
      │   ╱╱──── transition done poorly (or never)
      │──╱─────────────────→ Time
      Day 1            Day 90
```

Every day before break-even, you're a net consumer of organizational resources. Acceleration shortens this period.

## Key Principles

- **Match strategy to situation** — Use the STARS model (Start-up, Turnaround, Accelerated growth, Realignment, Sustaining success). A turnaround ≠ a start-up.
- **Learn before acting** — Have a systematic learning plan. Who knows what you need to know? Talk to them first.
- **Negotiate success** — Align on expectations with your boss explicitly. Don't assume alignment.
- **Secure early wins** — Build credibility through visible, meaningful results before attempting larger changes. See [[early-wins]].
- **Build coalitions** — Map the political landscape. Identify allies, fence-sitters, and resistors.
- **Avoid the "action imperative"** — The pressure to "do something" fast often leads to premature commitments. Learning *is* doing in the first 30 days.
