# Behavioral Blind Spots (The 20 Habits)

**Definition:** Interpersonal habits that hold leaders back from the next level of effectiveness — not because those leaders lack skill or intelligence, but because success has made the habits invisible. The leader's past success "proves" the behavior is fine, creating a feedback-resistant blind spot.

## Why It Matters

At the executive level, technical skills are a baseline. What differentiates leaders is **interpersonal behavior** — and the habits that sabotage this are almost always invisible to the person exhibiting them. Peers and reports notice them immediately; the leader is typically the last to know.

The paradox: the harder a leader worked to succeed, the more entrenched these habits become. Success is the problem. Every achievement confirms existing behavior; the brain strengthens the habit loop (see [[habit-loops]]).

## Goldsmith's 20 Habits

### Habits of Telling/Projecting
1. **Winning too much** — the need to win every argument, even trivial ones
2. **Adding too much value** — improving every idea presented (kills others' ownership)
3. **Passing judgment** — rating others and voicing the verdict
4. **Making destructive comments** — sarcasm or "just being honest" that wounds
5. **Starting with "No," "But," or "However"** — negating before engaging; makes others feel their input was irrelevant
6. **Telling the world how smart we are** — proving intelligence in every interaction

### Habits of Emotional Mismanagement
7. **Speaking when angry** — using emotional volatility as a management tool
8. **Negativity** — "Let me explain why that won't work"
9. **Punishing the messenger** — shooting the bearer of bad news (guarantees future silence)

### Habits of Withholding
10. **Withholding information** — not sharing context, credit, or relevance
11. **Failing to give proper recognition**
12. **Failing to express gratitude**
13. **Not listening**

### Habits of Self-Protection
14. **Making excuses** — explaining rather than owning
15. **Clinging to the past** — using history to deflect responsibility
16. **Refusing to express regret**
17. **Passing the buck**
18. **Claiming credit we don't deserve**

### Habits of Relational Failure
19. **Playing favorites**
20. **An excessive need to be "me"** — using personality as an excuse for harmful behavior

## The Success Delusion

The mechanism behind blind spots: when we succeed, we attribute success to our own behavior. Over time, this attribution becomes a conviction: "My behavior is fine because I succeeded." This is functionally unfalsifiable until external structured feedback breaks the loop (see [[feedforward]]).

## The Change Process

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Get feedback** | 360 structured feedback; identify the behavioral pattern |
| **Apologize** | Brief, unqualified — earns the right to change |
| **Advertise** | Tell people what you're working on — creates accountability |
| **Listen** | When people give input, say "thank you" — no defense |
| **Follow up** | Ask periodically: "Am I improving on X?" |
| **Feedforward** | Replace retrospective feedback with future-focused suggestions |
