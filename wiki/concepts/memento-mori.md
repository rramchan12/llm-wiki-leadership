# Memento Mori

**Definition:** Latin for "remember that you will die." A Stoic contemplative practice of regularly meditating on one's own mortality — not to generate despair, but as a **prioritization and presence tool**: if your time is finite and you know it, trivial concerns fall away and real priorities surface.

## Why It Matters

Most people act as if they have unlimited time, and their calendars, conversations, and choices reflect that assumption — filled with busywork, conflict, and deferred living. Memento mori is the corrective: **daily, intentional awareness of finitude generates the urgency that makes real prioritization possible.**

For leaders, it operates on two levels:
1. **Personal:** Am I spending this day, this role, this relationship in ways that matter to me?
2. **Organizational:** Are we working on what is genuinely important, or what feels safe and busy?

## The Stoic Argument

Seneca's opening letters (Epistulae Morales) are saturated with the claim that time is singular: *Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est* — "All things are foreign, Lucilius; time alone is truly ours." This is not pessimism; it is the most aggressive possible case for living deliberately.

Marcus Aurelius devotes substantial sections of Meditations to the observation that emperors, conquerors, and philosophers of the past are all equally dust — and that this should comfort rather than depress: it frees you from status-seeking and orients you toward present virtue.

## Practice Applications

| Context | Memento Mori Prompt |
|---------|-------------------|
| Morning check-in | "If this were my last month, would I spend today this way?" |
| Career decisions | "What would I regret not having done from this position?" |
| Conflict | "In 10 years, will this disagreement have mattered?" |
| Priority setting | "If I had one year of health remaining, what would I stop doing right now?" |
| Legacy | "What would I want to be true about how I led when I'm gone?" |
