# Procrastination Framework (ABCDE)

**Definition:** Tracy's systematic method for managing procrastination by deliberately choosing to delay low-value tasks while eliminating resistance to high-value ones — anchored by the ABCDE task-priority ranking and a disciplined "eat the frog first" habit.

## Why It Matters

Procrastination is not a moral failure — it is a near-universal feature of human cognition. The brain defaults to low-friction tasks that produce immediate feedback (inbox zero, small to-dos) rather than high-value work that is difficult, ambiguous, or long. Left unmanaged, this bias systematically underperforms every career.

Tracy's insight: the goal is not to eliminate procrastination but to **redirect it**. You will procrastinate on *something* every day. The only question is whether you procrastinate on your most important work (default) or on your least important work (intentional).

## The ABCDE Method

| Category | Definition | Action |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| **A** | Must do. Serious consequences if not done today. This is your frog. | Do first. If multiple A tasks, do A1 before A2. |
| **B** | Should do. Mild consequences if not done. | Do only after all A tasks complete |
| **C** | Nice to do. No real consequences. | Do only after A and B |
| **D** | Delegate. Could be done by someone else. | Pass it down and follow up |
| **E** | Eliminate. Not worth anyone's time. | Remove from list |

**The iron rule:** Never work on a B task while an A task exists undone.

The seduction of B tasks: they are often easier, more comfortable, and produce faster visible progress. This is precisely what makes them dangerous — they create the *feeling* of productivity while A tasks go undone.

## The Frog Metaphor

> "If it's your job to eat a live frog, it's best to do it first thing in the morning. And if it's your job to eat two frogs, eat the biggest one first."

The frog = your single most important, most avoided task. Start every day by doing it, before anything else. Before email. Before meetings. Before planning.

Why first thing in the morning? Two reasons:
1. **Willpower is finite.** Decision fatigue accumulates through the day; meeting the frog when energy is highest is when you're most likely to complete it.
2. **Psychological momentum.** Completing your highest-value task early creates a sense of accomplishment that makes everything else feel easier.

## The 80/20 Rule Applied to Tasks

20% of your tasks produce 80% of your results. The problem: the 80% low-value tasks are real work — they feel like real work, they produce visible output — they just don't produce the *important* output.

Discipline means constantly asking: "Is this task in my top 20%?" Most email, most meetings, most administrative work is not.

## Key Result Areas (KRAs)

Each role has 5–7 Key Result Areas — the non-negotiable outputs that define success:
1. Identify them explicitly
2. Rank them by impact
3. Look for your weakest KRA — it sets your performance ceiling
4. Focus development energy on the highest-impact KRA where you're weakest

This is personal Theory of Constraints: your weakest KRA is the individual's bottleneck. Improving it elevates overall performance; improving strong areas does not.

## Supporting Techniques

### Single-Handling
Once you begin your most important task, **don't stop until it's done.** Every stop-and-restart costs restart time plus context-loading time. Starting and stopping is the friction multiplier of procrastination.

### Creative Procrastination
Deliberately *choose* to delay low-value tasks to protect time for high-value tasks. This is the opposite of default procrastination: an intentional decision, made in advance, to deprioritize the unimportant.

### Prepare Thoroughly Before You Begin
The largest resistance point is often *starting*. Eliminate it by preparing everything in advance: clear desk, document open, materials gathered. Start-resistance dissolves when the environment is ready.

### Upgrade Your Skills Continuously
Procrastination often stems from **skill anxiety** — we avoid tasks we don't feel competent completing. The solution: invest in the skills your A tasks require. Competence reduces resistance.
