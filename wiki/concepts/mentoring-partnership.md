# Mentoring Partnership

**Definition:** A structured, intentional, two-way developmental relationship in which both mentor and protégé commit to goals, accountability, and reciprocal learning — not merely an informal arrangement where wisdom trickles downward.

## Why It Matters

Casual mentoring is everywhere and largely ineffective: senior people occasionally give advice; junior people occasionally take it; nothing is tracked; development is random. Intentional mentoring creates a reliable system where:
- The protégé develops faster
- The mentor gains fresh perspectives and renewed purpose
- The organization retains institutional knowledge

Blanchard's contribution is applying the same "simple, structured, humane" principle from [[one-minute-management]] to the mentoring relationship.

## The Five Phases

1. **Define purpose and goals** — what does the protégé want to achieve in 90 days? 12 months? 3 years? Goals must be specific, declared, and written.
2. **Build the relationship** — establish communication norms, psychological safety, and mutual expectations. How often do you meet? What's confidential? What kind of challenge is welcome?
3. **Determine the development plan** — structured learning activities, stretch assignments, reading, observation, coaching conversations
4. **Execute the plan** — the protégé does the work and reflects; the mentor provides input, challenge, and encouragement
5. **Wrap up and celebrate** — explicitly close the formal phase; articulate what was learned; discuss how the relationship continues informally

## Mutual Responsibilities

| Role | What They Provide |
|------|------------------|
| **Mentor** | Experience, perspective, honest challenge, network access, encouragement to take risks |
| **Protégé** | Written goals, preparation for every meeting, openness to feedback, action on what's learned, accountability |

**Both parties fail if either doesn't meet their responsibilities.** A protégé who repeatedly arrives unprepared or doesn't act on advice is disrespecting the mentor's investment. A mentor who gives vague advice and never challenges is failing the protégé.

## Being Both: The Dual Role Imperative

Blanchard argues that experienced leaders should always be in both a mentoring *and* a protégé relationship simultaneously:
- **As mentor:** Give back, stay connected to emerging challenges, gain fresh perspective
- **As protégé:** Keep growing, stay humble, access wisdom above your current level

Neither is optional once you're established. "You stop growing the day you stop being a protégé."

## Reverse Mentoring

A distinctive feature of the Blanchard model: protégés have things to teach their mentors, especially regarding:
- Technology and new tools
- Perspectives from different generations or backgrounds
- Fresh eyes on organizational problems the mentor no longer notices

Reverse mentoring normalizes the two-way flow and reduces the hierarchical tension that makes traditional mentoring awkward.
