# The WRAP Framework

**Definition:** A four-step decision-making process designed to counter the four predictable cognitive biases (the "Four Villains") that reliably cause bad decisions. Each letter corresponds to both an action and a villain it defeats.

| Step | Action | Villain it counters |
|------|--------|---------------------|
| **W** | Widen your options | Narrow framing |
| **R** | Reality-test your assumptions | Confirmation bias |
| **A** | Attain distance before deciding | Short-term emotion |
| **P** | Prepare to be wrong | Overconfidence |

---

## Why It Matters

Most decision frameworks focus on information gathering or cost-benefit analysis — they assume you've already framed the problem correctly. WRAP starts earlier: it corrects the framing *and* the information gathering *and* the emotional state *and* the planning horizon before a choice is locked in. It's a meta-process that makes other analysis more reliable.

---

## The Four Steps in Detail

### W — Widen Your Options
The goal is to escape the trap of binary ("should I or shouldn't I?") or artificially narrow choice sets.

**Key tools:**
- **Vanishing options test** — "If none of these options existed, what would I do?" Forces the mind out of the current frame.
- **Multi-tracking** — Deliberately pursue two options simultaneously. Reduces emotional attachment to any one path; faster information gathering.
- **Bright spots** — Who inside the organization has already solved a version of this problem?
- **Analogous situations** — Who outside has solved it? What can their experience tell us?
- **Ladder up/down** — Up: What's the deeper goal I'm trying to achieve? Down: What's the smallest possible step?

### R — Reality-Test Your Assumptions
The goal is to actively seek disconfirming information, not just validate the preferred option.

**Key tools:**
- **Consider the opposite** — Actively look for evidence that your preferred choice is wrong.
- **Devil's advocate** — Assign someone to argue against the frontrunner. Make disagreement structural, not personal.
- **Ooching** — Run small, cheap experiments before full commitment. "Don't just think — act your way into the answer."
- **Zoom out** — Consult base rates. What do people who have done this before actually report?
- **Zoom in** — Get direct experience. Visit, observe, talk to users. Don't rely on secondhand data alone.
- **"What would have to be true?"** — For each option, ask what underlying facts would need to hold for it to be correct. Then check those facts.

### A — Attain Distance Before Deciding
The goal is to override short-term emotion with medium- and long-term perspective.

**Key tools:**
- **10/10/10** — How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? The long view almost always clarifies.
- **Best-friend advice** — "What would I tell my best friend to do?" Removes you from the emotional fog of being the protagonist.
- **Core priorities** — Clarify your values and priorities *before* the decision arrives. Pre-decided principles are more reliable than in-the-moment reasoning.
- **"New CEO through the window"** — Andy Grove's Intel test: if a new CEO walked in, unburdened by history, what would they decide? Clears status quo and sunk-cost bias.

### P — Prepare to Be Wrong
The goal is to counteract overconfidence by planning for both failure and success scenarios.

**Key tools:**
- **Premortem** — "It's one year from now. We made this decision and it failed spectacularly. What happened?" Surfaces risks and dissent that politeness suppresses.
- **Pre-parade** — "It's one year from now. This worked brilliantly. What made it succeed?" Surfaces hidden opportunities.
- **Bookending** — Run both scenarios. The realistic range lies between the disaster and the breakthrough.
- **Tripwires** — Pre-set conditions that automatically trigger a decision review. Prevents sunk-cost escalation. ("If our market share drops below X, we revisit the strategy.")
- **Small bets** — Limit downside exposure while gathering information. Preserve optionality.
