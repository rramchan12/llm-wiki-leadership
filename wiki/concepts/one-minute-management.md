# One Minute Management

**Definition:** Blanchard and Johnson's three-part management model: One Minute Goals (clarity of expectations), One Minute Praisings (immediate specific positive feedback), and One Minute Reprimands/Redirects (immediate specific corrective feedback) — all brief, behavioral, and genuinely caring.

## Why It Matters

Most managers either avoid performance conversations entirely (letting problems fester) or hold annual reviews that summarize months of frustration in one overwhelming session. Both approaches fail. One Minute Management creates a continuous, low-intensity feedback loop that gives people what they need to succeed: clarity about what is expected, recognition when they get it right, and honest correction when they don't — immediately, specifically, personally.

The operating principle: **"People who feel good about themselves produce good results."** Management's job is not control — it is helping people feel capable, valued, and accountable.

## The Three Secrets

### 1. One Minute Goals

**What:** At the start of a project, role, or period, co-create written goals that:
- Can be read in one minute (no more than 250 words per goal)
- Include a clear description of the desired behavior/outcome
- Include a description of what good performance looks like

**How:**
1. Agree on the goals together (done *with* people, not *to* them)
2. Employee writes their own version and re-reads to confirm understanding
3. Review regularly — at least weekly

**Why it works:** Most performance problems are goal problems — the person didn't know what was expected. Clarity is the foundation. Self-managed people need goals, not surveillance.

### 2. One Minute Praisings

**What:** Catch people doing something right, and tell them immediately.

**Protocol:**
1. Tell them exactly what they did right (behavioral, specific — not vague)
2. Tell them genuinely how it makes you feel
3. Pause — let it land
4. Encourage more of it

**Why it works:** Managers who manage by exception (only intervene when something goes wrong) train their people that visibility = problem. Praisings reset this: good behavior is the default and attention follows it.

### 3. One Minute Reprimands / Re-Directs

**Protocol (in two halves):**

*First half:*
1. Address it immediately (not weeks later)
2. State specifically what happened (not a character judgment)
3. State how you feel about it (genuine, not theatrical)
4. Pause

*Second half:*
4. Affirm the person ("You're good at this job and I value you")
5. State that it's the behavior — not the person — that's the problem
6. Remind them you have confidence in them
7. The reprimand is over — do not carry it forward

**Why it works:** A clean, specific, immediate reprimand — followed immediately by genuine affirmation — is far less damaging and far more effective than accumulated resentment delivered as a broad character attack.

### The 2015 Update: One Minute Re-Directs

The revised model replaces "reprimand" with the gentler and more constructive **Re-Direct**, emphasizing:
- First, check whether your Goals were clear enough (often the manager's responsibility)
- Focus the correction on what better looks like, not what went wrong
- Collaborative coaching toward the goal

## Situational Leadership Context

Blanchard's broader framework (Situational Leadership) matches management style to the employee's development level:
- **Directing:** High task, low relationship — for people new to a task
- **Coaching:** High task, high relationship — for people developing confidence
- **Supporting:** Low task, high relationship — for people with skill but fluctuating motivation
- **Delegating:** Low task, low relationship (high autonomy) — for highly capable, committed performers

One Minute Management operates most naturally in the Coaching and Supporting quadrants — where the person needs feedback and recognition more than instruction or autonomy.
