# Four Villains of Decision-Making

**Definition:** The four predictable cognitive biases identified by Chip and Dan Heath that reliably cause bad decisions — not because of stupidity, but because of how human minds are wired. Understanding them is the first step in countering them.

---

## Why It Matters

Most decision-making advice focuses on process: gather more data, think longer, use a spreadsheet. But the four villains operate *before* analysis even begins — they shape what options we see, what information we seek, how we feel while choosing, and how much we trust our forecasts. Naming them is the prerequisite to applying the WRAP framework.

---

## The Four Villains

### 1. Narrow Framing
**What it is:** Defining the decision too narrowly — often as a binary choice ("should I do X or not?") — when the real question is "what are all the ways I could approach this goal?"

**How it manifests:**
- Treating a decision as "whether" rather than "which" or "how"
- Missing options that would obviously be visible if you stepped back
- The classic sign: you've deliberated for weeks but only considered two paths

**Counter:** Widen options (W step of WRAP). Vanishing options test. Multi-tracking.

---

### 2. Confirmation Bias
**What it is:** Seeking out information that confirms the option you already favor, while discounting or ignoring disconfirming evidence.

**How it manifests:**
- Consulting only people likely to agree with you
- Treating positive signals as strong evidence and negative signals as exceptions
- The "spectator problem" — watching the game you want to see, not the one being played

**Real-world example:** Quaker's acquisition of Snapple. The team was bullish and interpreted every piece of ambiguous data positively. Red flags (like distributor resistance) were rationalized away. The $1.7B acquisition was sold three years later for $300M.

**Counter:** Reality-test assumptions (R step of WRAP). Devil's advocate. Consider the opposite. Ooching.

---

### 3. Short-Term Emotion
**What it is:** Allowing current emotional state — excitement, fear, pride, anger — to distort the decision. Emotions that will feel trivial in a month or year feel overwhelming in the moment.

**How it manifests:**
- Accepting a bad offer because you're excited right now
- Avoiding a necessary confrontation because it's uncomfortable today
- Making a reactive decision after a setback that you'll regret once the dust settles

**Counter:** Attain distance (A step of WRAP). 10/10/10. Best-friend test. Core priorities.

---

### 4. Overconfidence
**What it is:** Systematically overestimating our ability to predict the future — how a market will develop, how a project will go, how a person will behave.

**How it manifests:**
- Under-planning for failure ("it'll work out")
- Setting timelines and budgets without contingencies
- Dismissing scenarios that seem unlikely (but aren't)

**Research backing:** Studies consistently show people's confidence intervals are far too narrow. Events they rate as "90% likely" happen only ~70% of the time.

**Counter:** Prepare to be wrong (P step of WRAP). Premortem. Tripwires. Bookending.
