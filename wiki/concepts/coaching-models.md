# Coaching Models

**Definition:** Structured frameworks for helping someone improve performance, develop capability, or resolve problems through guided questions, feedback, and accountability — rather than through direct instruction or telling.

## Why It Matters

Leaders default to telling: diagnosing the problem, prescribing the solution, and evaluating the result. This works for simple, clearly-understood tasks but fails for development, complex problem-solving, and leadership growth. Coaching inverts the dynamic: the leader asks rather than tells, draws out the person's own thinking, and holds them accountable for their own solutions.

The wiki's books converge on a central insight: **the most powerful interventions in leadership are questions, not answers.** Coaching is where this insight becomes a daily practice.

## Coaching Models in the Wiki

### 1. Blanchard's Situational Coaching (One Minute Management)

Blanchard's model embeds coaching within [[one-minute-management]]:

| Element | Coaching Application |
|---------|---------------------|
| **One Minute Goals** | Coach and person co-create clear, written goals — ensuring the person understands what success looks like before they start |
| **One Minute Praisings** | Coach catches good performance and reinforces immediately — the person learns what "right" looks like through specific recognition |
| **One Minute Re-Directs** | When performance drifts, the coach addresses it immediately: specific behavior → impact → reaffirmation of confidence → redirect toward the goal |

The 2015 update from "Reprimands" to "Re-Directs" reflects the evolution toward coaching: the manager first checks whether their *own goals* were clear enough, then collaboratively redirects rather than punishing.

### 2. Goldsmith's Feedforward Model

Goldsmith's [[feedforward]] replaces retrospective coaching (what went wrong) with future-focused coaching (what to do next):

| Traditional Feedback | Feedforward |
|---------------------|-------------|
| "Here's what you did wrong" | "Here's an idea for next time" |
| Judges the past | Suggests the future |
| Triggers defensiveness | Invites receptivity |
| Focuses on failure | Focuses on possibility |

**Protocol:** Ask: "What's one thing I could do better in [area]?" Receive the suggestion. Say "thank you." No defending, no explaining, no evaluating. The discipline of receiving without responding *is* the coaching.

### 3. Schein's Humble Inquiry as Coaching Stance

Schein's [[humble-inquiry]] provides the foundational coaching posture: genuine curiosity from a "one down" position. The four types of inquiry map directly to coaching conversations:

| Inquiry Type | Coaching Moment |
|-------------|-----------------|
| **Pure Humble Inquiry** | Opening: "What's on your mind?" "What's the real challenge here?" |
| **Diagnostic Inquiry** | Deepening: "What made that difficult?" "What did you feel when that happened?" |
| **Confrontational Inquiry** | Challenge: "Could it be that your approach to X is contributing to this?" |
| **Process Inquiry** | Meta: "How is this coaching conversation working for you?" |

The progression from pure to confrontational mirrors the coaching arc: start with the person's reality, then gradually introduce the coach's perspective.

### 4. Radical Candor's Care + Challenge Model

Scott's [[radical-candor]] provides the emotional calibration for coaching:

- **Care Personally** → The coach genuinely cares about the person's growth, not just their output
- **Challenge Directly** → The coach says the hard thing when the person needs to hear it

The four quadrants predict coaching failure modes:
- **Ruinous Empathy:** Coaching that avoids the hard truth — "You're doing great" when they're not
- **Obnoxious Aggression:** Coaching that's honest but uncaring — "This is terrible" without investment in the person
- **Manipulative Insincerity:** Going through coaching motions without genuine engagement

### 5. Covey's Stewardship Delegation

Covey's Habit 3 reframes delegation as coaching: specify the desired results and constraints, then let the person choose their own methods. The coach's role is to agree on accountability checkpoints, not to micromanage the approach.

This connects to development levels (see [[coachee-readiness]]): a person new to a task needs more method guidance; a skilled person needs only results and constraints.

### 6. The Arbinger Pyramid as Coaching Sequence

The Pyramid of Influence from [[arbinger-anatomy-of-peace]] provides the order of coaching operations:
1. Get out of the box (see the person as a person)
2. Build the relationship
3. Listen and learn
4. Teach and communicate
5. Correct (only if still needed)

Most bad coaching starts at step 5. The pyramid explains why: correction without relationship and listening is just punishment.

## The GROW Model (External Reference)

The GROW model (Goal → Reality → Options → Will) is the most widely taught coaching framework globally. While no wiki book defines it directly, the wiki's frameworks collectively reconstruct it:

| GROW Stage | Wiki Equivalent |
|-----------|----------------|
| **Goal** | One Minute Goals (Blanchard): what does success look like? |
| **Reality** | Humble Inquiry (Schein): what's actually happening? Seek First to Understand (Covey). |
| **Options** | Feedforward (Goldsmith): what could you do differently? |
| **Will** | Accountability (Blanchard + Covey): what will you do, by when, and how will we check? |

## Common Coaching Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | What Goes Wrong | Wiki Source |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Telling disguised as asking** | Rhetorical questions: "Don't you think you should…?" | [[humble-inquiry]] |
| **Solving instead of coaching** | Leader jumps to the answer rather than letting the person discover it | [[one-minute-management]] (Re-Directs, not Rescues) |
| **Coaching without caring** | The "coaching conversation" feels like a performance review | [[radical-candor]] (Obnoxious Aggression quadrant) |
| **Avoiding the hard truth** | Coach withholds honest assessment to protect the relationship | [[radical-candor]] (Ruinous Empathy quadrant) |
| **One-size-fits-all** | Same coaching approach regardless of the person's development level | [[one-minute-management]] (Situational Leadership) |
