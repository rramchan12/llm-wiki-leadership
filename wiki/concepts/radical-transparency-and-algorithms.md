# Radical Transparency and Algorithmic Decision-Making

**Definition:** Two interlocking Bridgewater practices developed by Ray Dalio: *Radical Transparency* — making reasoning, data, and disagreement maximally visible rather than managing information for political advantage — and *Algorithmic Decision-Making* — using explicit, pre-committed principles as decision rules to reduce the influence of bias, status, and in-the-moment emotion.

---

## Why It Matters

Most organizations run on implicit, unexamined principles enforced by whoever has the most power. Results:
- The most persuasive person wins rather than the person with the best reasoning
- Failures are concealed until too large to hide
- Lessons aren't institutionalized — the same mistakes recur
- Performance data is managed rather than used

Radical transparency + algorithmic decision-making is Dalio's antidote: a system where the quality of reasoning wins, failures are immediately visible and therefore correctable, and the organization improves from its mistakes faster than competitors.

---

## Radical Transparency

### What It Is
Making the reasoning behind decisions visible to everyone who is legitimately affected:
- Meeting recordings accessible to all employees
- Performance evaluations visible (the "dots" — scores on specific attributes for each person)
- Disagreements aired in real-time in the room, not in side conversations
- Error logs and root-cause analyses shared organizationally

### What It Is Not
Not: sharing *everything* regardless of relevance (information overload is not transparency)
Not: eliminating confidentiality where confidentiality is legitimately protective
Not: a culture of surveillance — it is a culture of *reasoning* being visible

### The Core Benefit
Radical transparency eliminates most of the political games that degrade organizational decision quality:
- No asymmetric information advantages (the basis of most organizational politics)
- No "hidden ball" maneuvering before the real conversation
- No ability to conceal poor reasoning or poor results

It also raises the stakes for intellectual honesty: if your reasoning is visible, you can't slide by on rhetorical skill alone.

### The Cost
Radical transparency is psychologically taxing, especially for people accustomed to more politically managed environments. It requires:
- Genuine ego discipline (your mistakes are visible)
- Organizational trust that transparency is used for learning, not for judgment or punishment
- Senior leadership modeling first — if leaders are selectively transparent, the system collapses

## Algorithmic Decision-Making

### The Problem It Solves
Humans make inconsistent decisions: the same choice looks different depending on how hungry you are, how recently you've experienced a failure, which person you're dealing with, or what the most recent headline was. This variability is not wisdom — it is noise. Noise produces bad outcomes even when average judgment is good.

Dalio's solution: where possible, codify decision rules as explicit algorithms ("if X properties are present, the decision should be Y") and enforce them consistently.

### Types of Algorithms

**Principle-based algorithms:**
- "If a person has failed to achieve their responsibilities in three out of five quarters, move them to a different role"
- "If a trade has lost more than [threshold]%, it must be reviewed by the risk committee within 24 hours"
- "If two senior managers with different views cannot resolve through discussion, escalate to believability-weighted vote"

**Hiring algorithms:**
- Role requires specific profile; profile is scored against defined criteria; criteria are applied consistently rather than by "gut feel"

**Post-mortem algorithms:**
- Every significant loss: structured failure analysis within [timeframe]
- Every major decision: log the reasoning so it can be reviewed against actual outcome

### Believability Weighting (The Key Innovation)
Not all inputs should be weighted equally. In a standard organization, the most senior person's view dominates. In a pure democracy, all views count equally. Dalio argues both are suboptimal.

Believability-weighted decision-making: each person's view is weighted by their *demonstrated track record* in the relevant domain. This creates a system where the best predictors have more influence — empirically, not by title.

Implementation:
- Track records are visible (the "dots" system)
- Disagreements are resolved partly by examining whose track record on this type of question is better
- New situations always require fresh judgment; historical credibility is a prior, not a determinant

## Principle-Building as Organizational Learning

The most durable application of algorithmic decision-making is principle-building: after every significant decision, outcome, or failure, ask "What principle, if codified, would produce better decisions on similar situations in the future?"

Over time, this produces an explicit organizational learning corpus — an operational manual that improves with every cycle. See [[dalio-principles]] for the full framework.
