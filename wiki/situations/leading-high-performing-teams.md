# Situation: Leading High-Performing Teams

**When this applies:** You are responsible for a team's performance and want to move from "functional" to "exceptional" — from a group that meets targets to a jelled unit that consistently exceeds them, self-directs, and sustains performance over time. Relevant whether you're inheriting a team, building one from scratch, or trying to elevate an existing one.

## What the Wiki Recommends

The wiki converges on a surprising finding: the key variables in high-performing teams are almost entirely sociological, not technical. Peopleware documents this empirically; Covey defines the values foundation; Blanchard provides the day-to-day practice. The manager's role is less to drive performance than to **create and protect the conditions in which performance emerges**.

Three layers of work:
1. **Foundation:** Psychological safety, clarity, trust
2. **Team chemistry:** Stable membership, shared purpose, completion experiences
3. **Individual development:** Continuous feedback, growth, mentoring

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Layer 1: Build the Foundation (Conditions)

1. **Establish psychological safety first**
   - People perform below their capability when they fear judgment, blame, or punishment for candor
   - See [[situations/creating-psychological-safety-through-questions]] for the full playbook
   - The leader's move: shift from telling to asking genuinely (see [[humble-inquiry]])

2. **Set goals with the team, not for the team**
   - One Minute Goals: team members write their own goals, re-state them, own them
   - Goals done *to* people create compliance; goals done *with* people create commitment
   - See [[one-minute-management]]

3. **Make the Emotional Bank Account explicit**
   - Is there accumulated trust in the team? Between you and each member?
   - High enough reserves mean conflict gets resolved; low reserves mean every friction escalates
   - See [[emotional-bank-account]]

4. **Build Circle of Influence clarity**
   - The team needs to understand what it controls, what it can influence, and what it must accept
   - Focusing team energy on the Circle of Influence (not Concern) prevents burnout and builds agency
   - See [[circle-of-influence]]

### Layer 2: Cultivate Team Chemistry (Jell)

5. **Protect team stability**
   - Don't reassign people mid-project; don't fragment attention across too many projects
   - Jell requires stability; it cannot form on a revolving door
   - See [[team-jell]]

6. **Create a shared goal the team cares about**
   - Not just a project deadline — something that connects to purpose
   - Teams that care about what they're building sustain effort through difficulty; teams that don't, drift

7. **Protect uninterrupted work time (E-factor)**
   - Schedule meeting-free blocks; shield the team from interruption during deep work
   - Cognitive work requires flow (see [[flow-states]]); constant interruption destroys it
   - The manager's job: absorb organizational noise so the team doesn't have to

8. **Create completion rituals**
   - Finishing something together is the most powerful team-bonding experience
   - Mark project completions; celebrate milestones; let teams see and own their work

### Layer 3: Develop Individuals Continuously

9. **Catch people doing things right — constantly**
   - Specific, immediate praisings are the highest-ROI management action
   - Most managers only react to problems; high-performing team leaders actively seek excellence to recognize

10. **Address problems immediately and cleanly**
    - One Minute Re-Direct: specific behavior + how you feel + affirmation of the person
    - Accumulated frustration delivered as a broad attack demoralizes and destroys trust

11. **Invest in development for high-potential members**
    - Mentoring partnerships; stretch assignments; explicit development goals
    - People don't stay where they don't grow
    - See [[mentoring-partnership]]

12. **Move toward interdependence deliberately**
    - Covey's highest maturity level: the team should eventually self-manage, challenge you, and produce results you couldn't specify in advance
    - The manager's goal is to make themselves less necessary as a director over time
    - See [[maturity-continuum]]

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Managing for heroism:** Rewarding individual contributors who go around the team destroys jell
- **Fragmentation:** Splitting team members across too many projects kills chemistry and reduces everyone's output
- **Optimizing cost per person over team output:** Peopleware's furniture police example — reducing workspace costs while destroying productivity
- **Ignoring the trust account:** Driving for results while depleting the relational substrate eventually collapses performance
- **Treating safety as a feelings issue:** Safety is a performance issue; it determines information flow and therefore decision quality
