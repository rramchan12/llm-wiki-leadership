# Situation: Soliciting Feedback from Your Manager and VP

**When this applies:** You want honest, actionable feedback from your direct manager or skip-level VP — not vague reassurance — and you want to create a sustainable loop where candor flows naturally upward.

## What the Wiki Recommends

Soliciting upward feedback is one of the highest-leverage leadership habits. Scott's Radical Candor makes it the **first order of operations** — solicit before you give. Goldsmith's research confirms that leaders who ask and follow up are rated as dramatically more effective. Yet most people do it poorly: they ask vague questions ("Any feedback?"), get vague answers ("You're doing fine"), and conclude their boss has nothing useful to share.

The fix is structural, not emotional. Use specific, future-focused questions; make it safe for the other person to be direct; receive without defending; and follow up to prove you meant it.

## Step-by-Step Approach

### 1. Ask a Specific, Future-Focused Question
Vague asks produce vague answers. Use one of these patterns:

- **Feedforward (Goldsmith):** "I'm working on becoming better at [X]. Can you give me two suggestions for what I could do differently going forward?"
- **Radical Candor (Scott):** "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?"
- **Business-impact framing (for VPs):** "What's the highest-leverage thing I should change to be more effective at [VP's priority]?"
- **Event-specific (for Managers in 1:1s):** "How did I handle [recent event]? What would you have done differently?"

### 2. Signal Genuine Intent (Mutual Purpose)
Per the Crucial Conversations framework, candor requires the other person to believe you genuinely want to grow — not that you're fishing for compliments or testing them.

Say it explicitly: "I'm asking because I genuinely want to get better, not because I'm looking for reassurance."

### 3. Adopt the "One-Down" Posture
Humble Inquiry (Schein) teaches that authentically admitting you need the other person's perspective builds trust and yields more accurate information. This is especially critical when asking upward — your VP/Manager likely defaults to "telling" mode. You need to pull them into candor by making it safe and easy to be direct.

### 4. Receive Without Defending
When they respond, say **"Thank you"** and stop. Do not:
- Defend: "The reason I did that was..."
- Evaluate: "I think that's unfair."
- Interpret: "You probably misunderstood the context."
- Probe defensively: "Who else said that?"

Goldsmith's golden rule: the moment you justify, you've taught them never to be honest with you again.

### 5. Follow Up at 30-Day Intervals
Circle back: "Last month you suggested I [X]. Have you noticed any improvement? Anything else I should adjust?"

Goldsmith's research shows that leaders who follow up are rated as dramatically more improved — even when actual behavior change is identical. The follow-up signal itself creates ongoing psychological safety for future candor.

### 6. Tailor to the Relationship

| Audience | Approach |
|----------|----------|
| **Direct Manager** (high trust, frequent contact) | Informal; embed in regular 1:1s; ask about specific recent events |
| **VP / Skip-Level** (lower frequency, higher stakes) | Structured; pick one topic; frame around business impact; don't waste their political capital on vague asks |

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Vague "any feedback?" asks** — invites "you're doing fine" and teaches them you don't really want candor
- **Defending or explaining** when they do share — kills future honesty instantly
- **Asking once and never following up** — signals the conversation was performative
- **Asking in a group setting** — upward feedback requires privacy and psychological safety
- **Fishing for compliments disguised as feedback requests** — detected immediately; destroys trust
- **Asking during a stressful moment** — choose a calm 1:1 context, not a crisis
