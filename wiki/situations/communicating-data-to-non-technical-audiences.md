# Situation: Communicating Data and Numbers to Non-Technical Audiences

**When this applies:** You have data, analysis, or statistics that support an important decision, strategy, or recommendation — but your audience is not numerically fluent, lacks context, or is making decisions from the gut. You need the numbers to land, not just be reported.

---

## What the Wiki Recommends

The root failure is treating communication as information transfer. Numbers are not self-evidently meaningful — they require translation into human experience. The goal is not to share data accurately; it is to make the data *felt*.

---

## Step-by-Step Approach

### 1. Identify the one number that matters most
Every complex analysis has a core quantity. Find it and lead with it. Round everything else aggressively. Use [[ruthless-prioritization]] on your data points — what is the Commander's Intent-equivalent for this dataset?

### 2. Translate with a concrete comparison
Anchor the core number in something the audience already knows — a physical object, a time duration, a spatial scale, a grocery bill.
- If it's large: scale it to something the audience can picture (a football field, a year of commutes, a household budget)
- If it's small: scale it up ("if this were your annual salary, you'd be earning $0.23 a year")
- If it's a rate: convert to a frequency ("once every 47 seconds")

See [[numeracy-translation]] for the full toolkit.

### 3. Reduce to human scale
Divide aggregate numbers by the relevant unit. National, organizational, or global statistics are almost always more actionable as per-person, per-day, or per-event figures.

### 4. Apply the One-Person Rule for human impact
When numbers represent people, zoom from the statistic to one specific individual. Name them, describe their situation in one concrete sentence. One person the audience can imagine is worth 10x the data.

### 5. Proportion over absolute
When presenting comparisons, use proportional frames: "1 in 4," "half the audience," "twice as likely." Reserve absolute figures for anchoring, not persuasion.

### 6. Create a curiosity gap (then close it)
Lead with a counterintuitive or surprising number — then satisfy the gap with the translation. This uses the Unexpected principle from [[sticky-communication]] to ensure the audience is cognitively engaged before the payload lands.

### 7. Tell a mini-scenario
If the number is about a process or system, translate it into a brief experiential narrative — a sequence of events the audience can mentally simulate. This activates the Simulation function of story.

---

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Precision theater:** Adding decimal places signals rigor to you; it signals noise to the audience. Round to what is actionable.
- **Fire-hosing:** Presenting all the data so the audience sees you've done the work. They won't feel anything from a table of 20 numbers. Cut 80%.
- **Assuming shared context:** "Our NPS dropped 12 points" lands only if the audience knows what NPS is and what 12 points means. Never skip the anchor.
- **Statistics without a face:** Numbers about people without a human story are systematically under-persuasive.
- **Confusing accuracy with impact:** A perfectly accurate number that cannot be felt is useless for decision-making.
