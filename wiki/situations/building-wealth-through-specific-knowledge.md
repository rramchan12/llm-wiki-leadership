# Situation: Building Wealth and Leverage Through Specific Knowledge and Compounding

**When this applies:** You are at a career inflection point — considering whether to stay in a credentials-based, time-for-money career track, or shift toward building specific knowledge and leverage. You want a framework for thinking about wealth creation that goes beyond "work harder" or "invest in index funds."

## What the Wiki Recommends

Naval Ravikant's framework is the most coherent systematic answer to "how do you get rich without getting lucky?" that this wiki contains. The core insight: **stop selling time; start building ownership.** This requires moving from the generic (skills anyone can be trained for) to the specific (your unique intersection of curiosity, skill, and experience), and then deploying leverage (code, media, capital, labor) to multiply your output beyond what time alone can achieve.

This is not a quick framework. It requires 10–20 year compounding. But the alternative — linear career progression in traded-time roles — has a hard ceiling and no compounding.

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Identify Specific Knowledge
1. **Follow genuine curiosity, not market demand.** What do you obsessively read about without anyone telling you to? What do you know that your colleagues don't — and that you arrived at sideways, not through training? [[specific-knowledge]]

2. **Map your unusual skill stack.** You don't need to be #1 in one thing. Are you in the top 15–20% in 2–3 things that rarely co-occur? The combination is where specific knowledge lives.

3. **Test the replaceability question.** Could your employer train someone else to do your most valuable work in 6 months? If yes, that's not specific knowledge — it's a commodity role. If no, you're closer to the target.

### Phase 2: Build Leverage
4. **Choose your primary leverage type ([[leverage]]):**
   - *Code*: Build software that works while you sleep. Permission-less and zero marginal cost.
   - *Media*: Write, teach, or make content that scales ideas without your presence.
   - *Capital*: If you have track record, invest your own money/knowledge in assets.
   - *Labor*: If you manage people, train them to multiply your specific knowledge.

5. **Prefer permission-less leverage first** (code, media) because it doesn't require track record or capital. Build it while you still have a salary.

6. **"Productize yourself":** Identify the atomic unit of your specific knowledge. Package it as a product, a service, a framework, or content. Move from "I consult on this" to "I have a thing for this."

### Phase 3: Compound and Protect
7. **Play long-term games with long-term people.** Every relationship, reputation, and skill compounds when treated as long-term. Short-term optimization destroys compounding. [[jorgenson-naval-almanack]]

8. **Accountability and reputation.** Put your name on your work. Be scrupulously honest. Your reputation functions as type-4 luck: it attracts opportunities without you hunting them.

9. **Reinvest in specific knowledge.** Keep following curiosity into the reading and thinking that deepens your irreplaceable edge. The advantage compounds when continued; it stagnates when exploited.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Chasing trends, not curiosity:** Every specific-knowledge domain that is fashionable gets crowded and commoditizes. Follow what is genuinely interesting to you, which is less likely to be crowded.
- **Leverage without specific knowledge:** Leverage without differentiation amplifies generic work — the market pays you like the commodity you are.
- **Impatience:** This framework requires at least a decade of compounding before most people see major results. The people who fail typically abandon the approach during the 5-year drought before the compound curve kicks.
- **Status games ([[resisting-status-games]]):** Status-seeking and genuine wealth-building are often in tension. Status drives you toward visible, high-prestige credentials; specific knowledge is often niche, eccentric, and low-prestige until it suddenly isn't.
