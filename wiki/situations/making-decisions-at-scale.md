# Situation: Making Decisions at Scale Using Explicit Principles

**When this applies:** You lead a team, department, or organization that is large enough that you cannot personally be in every important decision. Inconsistency is emerging — different managers deciding similar situations differently, culture drift is occurring, the implicit principles of the organization don't reflect the explicit ones. Or you are building a new organization and want to establish principled decision-making from the start.

---

## What the Wiki Recommends

The problem with implicit culture — the default — is not that the principles are wrong but that they are inconsistent, unexamined, and invisible. When they're implicit, the real principles are whatever the most powerful person in the room happens to believe that day. Making them explicit, debatable, visible, and enforced through mechanisms converts culture from "default" to "designed."

This playbook draws from Dalio's principle-building system, Amazon's mechanism-enforcement approach, and Willink's accountability architecture.

---

## Step-by-Step Approach

### 1. Surface the implicit principles you already use
Before writing new principles, identify the ones you already apply. For 2–3 recent significant decisions:
- What reasoning led to that decision?
- What, generalized, would the principle be?
- Would you apply it consistently in similar cases?

This reverse-engineering of existing practice is both faster than starting from scratch and more honest about actual (vs. aspirational) culture.

### 2. Write them specifically
Every principle must be specific enough that someone could self-evaluate against it in a performance review without your presence.

**Too vague:**
- "Be accountable"
- "Prioritize the customer"

**Specific enough:**
- "If your team misses a significant milestone, your first diagnostic question is: 'What did I fail to provide?' — not 'Who dropped the ball?'"
- "Before committing resources to any initiative, write a Press Release describing the customer benefit. If you cannot write it compellingly, the initiative is not ready."

See [[leadership-principles-as-operating-system]].

### 3. Prioritize when they conflict
Principles will conflict. You must decide in advance which one wins. If you don't, the loudest person in the room decides — which is implicit culture again.

Example: "Move fast" vs. "Get it right" — what is your tie-breaker? Under what conditions does speed win? Under what conditions does precision win?

Dalio's approach: write explicit principles for the conflict cases, not just the common cases.

### 4. Enforce through mechanisms, not exhortation
Principles only change behavior when they have teeth. What happens when someone violates a principle?

Mechanisms (Amazon/Bridgewater examples):
- Hiring: interview questions directly evaluate against named principles; Bar Raiser can block a hire that doesn't meet specific principles
- Performance reviews: principle scores are explicit and tracked
- Promotion criteria: principled behavior is a requirement, not a bonus
- Onboarding: principles are taught with examples in week one

If the only enforcement is "the leader sometimes mentions it at all-hands," the principle is decorative. See [[leadership-principles-as-operating-system]].

### 5. Make reasoning visible (selective radical transparency)
The most durable mechanism: make the *reasoning* behind significant decisions visible to the people affected. This is not sharing everything — it is sharing the logic behind consequential choices.

Benefits:
- Teaches the principles through real examples (far more effective than description)
- Reduces "why was that decided?" overhead
- Creates accountability for the quality of reasoning, not just the outcome

See [[radical-transparency-and-algorithms]].

### 6. Build the principle library from failures
After every significant failure (and success), hold a structured debrief: what happened, what was the root cause, what principle would have prevented this? The best organizations build their principle libraries this way — each entry is backed by a real case with known outcome. See [[premortem]] for the upstream version.

### 7. Audit and update deliberately
Principles can become outdated. Schedule an annual review: are the stated principles still the right ones? Which have been violated without consequence (revealing they are aspirational, not operative)? Which have produced unintended behaviors?

Principles that are enforced but no longer represent organizational wisdom must be updated. Principles that are stated but not enforced must either be enforced or retired.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Too many principles:** Amazon started with many and converged to 14 highly specific ones. Bridgewater's 210+ work for a specialized financial organization but would overwhelm most. Start with 5–10.
- **Principles as wall posters:** If they are not in the hiring criteria, performance review, and leadership behavior, they are decoration.
- **Believing uniformity:** Different roles and situations may need different principle weighting. Context matters. Principles are not algorithms that override judgment — they are better-than-default heuristics.
- **Principle that protects leaders:** Watch for implicit principles that punish the team for what the leader should own. "Hold people accountable" is a leadership principle; it is not an excuse to deflect the leader's accountability to others.
