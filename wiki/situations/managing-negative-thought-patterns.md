# Situation: Managing Negative Thought Patterns

**When this applies:** You're caught in recurring negative thoughts — self-criticism, worry, rumination, catastrophizing — that are dragging your mood down, sapping your energy, or paralyzing your decision-making.

---

## What the Wiki Recommends

Three complementary approaches, each attacking the problem from a different angle. Use whichever resonates — or use all three in sequence.

---

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Tier 1 — CBT: Challenge the Content (Burns)
*Best when you can identify specific distorted thoughts.*

1. **Catch the thought.** When your mood shifts, ask: "What just went through my mind?"
2. **Write it down verbatim.** Don't edit or rationalize.
3. **Identify the distortion.** Which of Burns's 10 [[cognitive-distortions]] applies? (All-or-nothing? Emotional reasoning? Should statements?)
4. **Write a rational response.** Not "positive thinking" — *accurate* thinking. What would the evidence actually support?
5. **Re-rate your mood.** It usually improves even after one round.

### Tier 2 — ACT: Defuse from the Thought (Harris)
*Best when the thought keeps returning even after you've challenged it, or when the problem is avoidance.*

1. **Notice and name.** "I'm having the thought that I'm not good enough."
2. **Defuse.** Use a [[cognitive-defusion]] technique: silly voice, "thanks mind," leaves on a stream.
3. **Accept the feeling.** Don't try to make the discomfort go away. Make room for it.
4. **Choose toward values.** Ask: "What would I do right now if this thought had no power over me?" Then do that. See [[values-based-action]].

### Tier 3 — Habit-Loop: Update the Reward (Brewer)
*Best when negative thinking has become chronic and automatic.*

1. **Map the loop.** Trigger (uncertainty, setback) → behavior (worry, rumination) → reward (??).
2. **Test the reward.** "How rewarding is this worrying *right now*?" Rate honestly. You'll likely find: not rewarding at all.
3. **Disenchantment.** Let the brain register that worry doesn't deliver. No willpower needed.
4. **The BBO.** Replace with curiosity: "Hmm, what does this anxiety actually feel like in my body?" See [[habit-loops]].

---

## When to Use Which

| Situation | Best Approach |
|---|---|
| Identifiable distorted thought causing acute mood drop | **Tier 1 (Burns/CBT)** — challenge the content |
| Thought has been challenged but keeps returning; avoidance is the pattern | **Tier 2 (Harris/ACT)** — defuse and act on values |
| Chronic, automatic worry/rumination that feels like "who you are" | **Tier 3 (Brewer/Habit-loop)** — update the reward value |
| All three at once | Often the most effective long-term approach |

---

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Turning thought-challenging into thought-fighting.** Burns's technique is about accuracy, not positivity. If the negative thought is accurate, the response isn't to deny it — it's to respond proportionately.
- **Using defusion to suppress.** Defusion isn't avoidance with a mindfulness label. You're making room for the thought, not pushing it away.
- **Expecting instant results from habit-loop work.** The brain updates over repetitions, not in one session. Be patient.
- **Skipping feeling identification.** All three approaches start with *noticing* — what am I feeling, what am I thinking? Many people skip this step.
