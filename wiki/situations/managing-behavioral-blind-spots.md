# Situation: Managing Behavioral Blind Spots in Leaders

**When this applies:** A leader is producing good results but damaging relationships, stifling their team, or impeding their own advancement — and they don't fully see the pattern. The problem is behavioral, not technical. Often flagged by 360 feedback, team attrition, low candor from direct reports, or feedback from a coach or peer.

## What the Wiki Recommends

The root cause is almost always the **success delusion** (Goldsmith): the leader's past success has made them misattribute bad behaviors as causes of success rather than obstacles to the next level. The fix requires structured external feedback, a genuine apology, behavioral change advertised to key stakeholders, and a shift to **feedforward** (future-focused suggestion intake) rather than backward-looking criticism.

Deeper cases — where the behavior reflects a fundamentally objectifying view of colleagues — require the Arbinger layer: not just changing what you do, but how you see.

## Step-by-Step Approach

1. **Surface the pattern with structured feedback**
   - Run a 360 with behavioral specificity, not trait ratings
   - Look for patterns across multiple relationships — a single piece of feedback may be noise; a pattern is signal
   - Present findings descriptively, not as moral judgment

2. **Identify the specific habit(s) from Goldsmith's 20**
   - The point isn't labeling — it's pattern recognition
   - Priority: which 1–2 habits are causing the most damage to relationships?
   - See [[behavioral-blind-spots]]

3. **Apologize — briefly and without qualification**
   - "I've been listening to feedback. I know I've been [X]. I'm going to work on it."
   - No "but." No "I was just trying to..."
   - This earns the credibility to change

4. **Advertise the behavioral commitment**
   - Tell direct reports and key peers: "I'm working on [specific behavior]. Please call me on it when you see it."
   - Creates accountability and signals psychological safety for future feedback

5. **Shift to feedforward**
   - Ask specific stakeholders: "Can you give me two suggestions for how I can be better at [X] in the future?"
   - Listen; say "thank you"; don't defend or explain
   - See [[feedforward]]

6. **Follow up regularly**
   - At 30-day intervals, ask the same people: "Am I improving on X?"
   - The follow-up signal itself changes behavior: leaders who ask demonstrably improve faster

7. **Check the deeper layer (if needed)**
   - If the behavior is rooted in seeing others as obstacles or inferiors, behavioral change alone won't hold
   - Use Arbinger's collusion diagnosis: "Am I in the box?" (see [[self-deception]])

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **One-time feedback session without follow-up:** Feedback without follow-up decays to nothing
- **Defending the feedback:** Arguing with 360 data signals that the process was performative; destroys trust
- **Big broad behavior targets:** "Be a better listener" is too vague to act on; "don't speak for 3 minutes after someone finishes" is actionable
- **Treating it as a performance problem:** Behavioral blind spots are not performance deficiencies — they're success-induced habits. Don't frame it punitively.
- **Expecting fast change:** Interpersonal habits change over months, not days. Follow-up is essential.
