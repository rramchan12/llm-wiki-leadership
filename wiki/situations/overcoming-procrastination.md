# Situation: Overcoming Procrastination

**When this applies:** You consistently delay your most important work while staying busy with lower-value tasks. You end too many days having expended effort but not having moved the needle. You know what you should do and reliably don't do it. The fear, discomfort, or complexity of the important task drives you to the comfort of the trivial.

## What the Wiki Recommends

Procrastination is not a character flaw — it is a habit loop (Brewer) and a cognitive default (Tracy). The brain prefers tasks with immediate feedback and low threat over tasks with delayed, uncertain payoff. This is the natural state. The work is not to become a different person, but to build structures that make the important task easier to start than to avoid.

Three complementary interventions: **structural** (Tracy's ABCDE + environment design), **motivational** (Covey's Q2 and values alignment), and **psychological** (Brewer/Harris: discomfort as a signal to investigate, not to flee).

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Identify the Frog (Before Today Ends)

1. **Write down all tasks for tomorrow** — list everything, no filtering
2. **Apply ABCDE:**
   - A tasks = significant consequences; your frogs (your A1 is the **biggest** frog)
   - B tasks = mildly important; do after A tasks complete
   - C tasks = nice-to-do; no real consequence
   - D = delegate now
   - E = eliminate
3. **Identify your A1 task** — this is tomorrow's first priority, non-negotiable
4. See [[procrastination-framework]]

### Phase 2: Prepare the Environment (Before Bed or Before Leaving Office)

5. **Clear your workspace** — remove clutter and distraction from the space where you'll work
6. **Open or prepare your A1 task** — have the document, tool, or materials ready before you start
7. **Block the first 60–90 minutes** — do not schedule meetings before the frog is eaten

The preparation eliminates start-resistance. The frog becomes hardest in the abstract; prepared environments make starting frictionless.

### Phase 3: Eat the Frog First

8. **Do not open email or social media first** — email and messages are other people's agendas for your time
9. **Start your A1 task cold** — the first five minutes are the hardest; commit to five minutes minimum
10. **Single-handle** — work on it continuously until done or a predetermined stopping point; resist every urge to switch
11. **No task-switching** — context-switching multiplies cost; stay on one task

### Phase 4: If You're Stuck (Psychological Layer)

12. **Name the feeling, don't flee it**
    - "I notice I'm avoiding this. What's the feeling underneath?"
    - Often: fear of failing, fear of judgment, uncertainty about how to proceed
    - Curiosity about the discomfort is less paralyzing than avoidance
    - See [[cognitive-defusion]] and the RAIN technique ([[RAIN]])

13. **Use the 10-10-10 test** (Decisive)
    - How will I feel about having avoided this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?
    - Temporal distance reveals the stakes more clearly than the immediate emotional discomfort

14. **Connect to values** — why does this task matter? Which of your values does it serve?
    - When tasks feel meaningless, the problem is often values disconnection, not laziness
    - See [[values-based-action]]

### Phase 5: Build the Long-Term Habit

15. **Protect Q2 time**
    - Schedule one 2-hour block of protected deep work daily
    - Decline Q3 commitments that colonize this time
    - See [[time-management-matrix]]

16. **Celebrate completion** — recognize when you eat the frog; the reward system needs a signal
    - Brief physical break, explicit acknowledgment, progress journal entry
    - The brain needs to associate the frog-eating with reward, not just relief

17. **Track over time** — a simple done list showing progress on A tasks builds momentum and identity
    - "I'm someone who does hard work first" is a powerful habit identity

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Inbox first:** Clearing email before the A task is the most common procrastination disguise — it feels productive while guaranteeing the A task never gets priority focus
- **Planning as procrastination:** Organizing your task list for the third time *is* procrastination if the frog remains uneaten
- **All-or-nothing thinking:** "I can't start the report until I have the full analysis" — start with what you have; momentum generates more momentum
- **Perfectionism before starting:** A B+ started is infinitely better than an A+ imagined
