# Situation: Facing a Major Decision

**When to use this playbook:** You're facing a high-stakes choice — a strategic pivot, a hire, a major investment, a career change — and you want to reduce the chance of a costly mistake.

---

## The Problem

When the stakes are high, our cognitive biases become most dangerous. We frame too narrowly ("should I or shouldn't I?"), seek only confirming evidence, feel the weight of the moment emotionally, and overestimate how well we can predict outcomes. The standard advice — "think harder" or "trust your gut" — doesn't address these systematic failures.

---

## The Playbook

### Step 1 — Diagnose the Villain First
Before applying any tool, ask: which of the [[four-villains-of-decision-making]] is most likely active here?

| Symptom | Likely Villain | Go to Step |
|---------|---------------|------------|
| You've only considered 1–2 options | Narrow framing | Step 2 |
| You've already "decided" and are seeking validation | Confirmation bias | Step 3 |
| You're dreading the decision emotionally right now | Short-term emotion | Step 4 |
| Your plan has no risk scenarios or fallback | Overconfidence | Step 5 |

---

### Step 2 — Widen Your Options
*(Counter: Narrow framing)*

1. Apply the **vanishing options test**: "If neither of my current options were available, what would I do?" Write down 3 answers.
2. Try **multi-tracking**: Can you pursue two options in parallel for a limited time before committing?
3. Ask: **Who has already solved this?** Identify 2–3 people or organizations with analogous experience and talk to them directly.
4. **Ladder up**: What's the deeper goal you're trying to achieve? Are your current options even aimed at it?

**Exit criterion:** You have at least 3 meaningfully different options on the table before moving to Step 3.

---

### Step 3 — Reality-Test Your Assumptions
*(Counter: Confirmation bias)*

1. For each option, ask: **"What would need to be true for this to be the right choice?"** Write down the key assumptions.
2. Identify the **most falsifiable** of those assumptions and actively seek evidence that they're wrong.
3. **Assign a devil's advocate.** Ask someone who disagrees with your frontrunner to make their best case. Don't rebut — just listen and capture.
4. If possible, **ooch**: design a small, cheap experiment that will produce real information before you commit. What's the minimum viable test?
5. **Check base rates**: What do people who have done this before actually report? What's the outside view?

**Exit criterion:** You have actively sought at least one credible source of disconfirming information for your preferred option.

---

### Step 4 — Attain Distance
*(Counter: Short-term emotion)*

1. Run the **10/10/10**: How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? Write the answers separately.
2. Ask yourself: **"What would I tell my best friend to do in this exact situation?"** Write that advice down without filtering.
3. Check against **core priorities**: Does each option honor what you said mattered most *before* this decision arrived? (If you haven't pre-defined your priorities, do that now as a first step.)
4. Apply the **"new CEO" test**: If a new leader walked in tomorrow with no prior investment in any of these options, what would they choose?

**Exit criterion:** You can articulate why your leading option is consistent with your long-term priorities, not just how you feel right now.

---

### Step 5 — Prepare to Be Wrong
*(Counter: Overconfidence)*

1. Run a **[[premortem]]** on your leading option: "It's one year from now. We made this choice and it failed significantly. What happened?" (Write individually before discussing as a group.)
2. Run a **pre-parade**: "It's one year from now. This succeeded brilliantly. What made it work?"
3. **Bookend the future**: Identify the realistic disaster scenario and the realistic breakthrough scenario. Your plan should address both.
4. Set at least one **[[tripwire]]**: "If [specific condition] occurs by [specific date], we will stop and reassess." Assign someone to monitor it.

**Exit criterion:** You have a documented tripwire and at least one mitigation for the most likely failure mode identified in the premortem.

---

## Quick Reference: When You're Short on Time

If you only have 15 minutes, run the **3-question triage**:

1. "What if none of my current options were available?" — *forces option expansion*
2. "What would I tell a friend to do?" — *creates emotional distance*
3. "If this fails in a year, what was the most likely cause?" — *mini-premortem*

These three questions address all four villains with minimal time investment.
