# Situation: Delegating and Developing Direct Reports

**When this applies:** You are a manager who needs to get work done through others while simultaneously developing those people for greater responsibility. You're either over-directing (can't let go), under-directing (delegating without clarity), or not developing at all (execution-only mode). Good delegation is one of the highest-leverage skills in management.

## What the Wiki Recommends

The foundational insight from Blanchard: **most people know whether their work is good or bad only after annual reviews — far too late to self-correct.** Effective delegation requires three things working together: clear goals someone can check themselves against, immediate recognition when they get it right, and immediate (behavioral) correction when they don't.

Covey adds the interdependence layer: the goal of development is to produce capable, self-directed people who can eventually operate independently — and then collaborate as peers. Delegation done well creates human capital; done poorly, it creates learned helplessness or mistrust.

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Calibrate the Person

1. **Assess development level for this specific task** (Situational Leadership)
   - Low skill/high commitment (new to the task): needs direction
   - Some skill/lower confidence (developing): needs coaching and encouragement
   - High skill/variable motivation: needs support, not direction
   - High skill/high commitment: delegate fully with periodic check-ins

2. **Diagnose what's missing**
   - Is the performance problem a Skills problem? (Need practice, training, clear instruction)
   - Is it a Will problem? (Need motivation, clarity on consequences, recognition)
   - Never treat a skills problem as a will problem, or vice versa

### Phase 2: Set One Minute Goals Together

3. **Co-write clear goals for the delegated work**
   - The goal should be readable in under a minute
   - State the desired output and what good performance looks like
   - Have the person re-state it in their own words — ensure actual alignment
   - See [[one-minute-management]]

4. **Agree on check-in frequency based on development level**
   - New to task: more frequent
   - High-performer: less frequent; but never zero

### Phase 3: Catch Them Doing Things Right

5. **Immediately recognize good work**
   - Specific, behavioral, immediate
   - "You just [behavior]. That made [impact]. I appreciate it."
   - Avoid vague: "Great job!" — they don't know what to repeat
   - See One Minute Praisings

### Phase 4: Redirect When Needed

6. **Address problems immediately and specifically**
   - Don't accumulate — the longer the gap, the more weighted the conversation becomes
   - Focus on the behavior, not the person
   - First half: what happened and how you feel. Second half: affirm their value and your confidence.

### Phase 5: Invest in Development (Beyond Task)

7. **Start a mentoring thread for high-potential reports**
   - Even informally: "I'd like to help you grow toward [goal]. Can we talk about that?"
   - Define 90-day development goals together
   - Share your network, perspective, and honest challenge
   - See [[mentoring-partnership]]

8. **Use Habit 5 (Seek First to Understand) in every development conversation**
   - Before prescribing, listen — what does the person see as their growth edge?
   - Their assessment matters: they are more committed to goals they generated
   - See [[humble-inquiry]]

### Phase 6: Move Toward Interdependence

9. **Build the relationship so the person can challenge you**
   - Delegation that creates only downward dependency doesn't develop leaders
   - Encourage them to push back on you when they have better information
   - A developing leader who can disagree with you respectfully is a success sign, not a problem

10. **Celebrate growth explicitly**
    - When they graduate to higher autonomy or responsibility: mark it
    - Recognition at transitions is one of the defining moments (see [[defining-moments]]) that make development memorable and motivating

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Dumping without clarity:** "Here, you handle this" without goals, context, or support = setting up to fail
- **Rescuing too quickly:** Jumping in to take back a task when the person struggles prevents them from developing
- **Only redirecting, never praising:** Builds a relationship where visibility = problem
- **Not developing at all:** Good execution today; no bench strength tomorrow
