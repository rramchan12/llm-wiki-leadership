# Situation: Breaking Anxiety Cycles

**When this applies:** You're trapped in an anxiety cycle — worry leading to avoidance, avoidance leading to more anxiety, anxiety leading to more worry. The cycle feels automatic and inescapable.

---

## What the Wiki Recommends

Anxiety cycles are self-reinforcing [[habit-loops]]. The way out is not willpower, distraction, or reassurance — it's understanding the loop, updating its reward value, and introducing a genuinely better alternative. Three books converge on this.

---

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Step 1 — Map Your Loop (Brewer, First Gear)
Write down:
- **Trigger:** What started the anxiety? (Uncertainty, a thought, a situation, a physical sensation)
- **Behavior:** What are you doing right now? (Worrying, avoiding, doom-scrolling, stress-eating, seeking reassurance, over-preparing)
- **"Reward":** What does this behavior give you? (Usually: illusion of control, temporary distraction, but at the cost of more anxiety)

### Step 2 — Test the Reward (Brewer, Second Gear)
Ask yourself honestly: "How rewarding is this behavior *right now*, on a scale of 1–10?"

You'll typically find:
- Worrying: feels productive, delivers nothing (reward: 2/10)
- Avoidance: provides relief, shrinks your world (net negative)
- Reassurance-seeking: calms you temporarily, reinforces the loop (net negative)

**This isn't cognitive challenging** — it's experiential. Feel the truth in your body.

### Step 3 — Apply the Bigger Better Offer (Brewer, Third Gear)
Replace the anxious behavior with **curiosity**:
- "Hmm, what does this anxiety actually feel like in my chest?"
- "Is it getting bigger or smaller as I pay attention?"
- "What shape is it? What temperature?"

Curiosity activates the same reward pathway as worry — but actually delivers. It's incompatible with anxiety.

### Step 4 — Defuse and Act on Values (Harris)
If the anxiety attaches to specific thoughts:
1. "I'm having the thought that something bad will happen."
2. "Thanks, mind, for trying to protect me."
3. "What would I do right now if this anxiety had no power over my choices?"
4. Do that thing. Committed action, even small, breaks the avoidance cycle.

### Step 5 — Reality-Check Catastrophic Thoughts (Burns)
If the anxiety is driven by a specific cognitive distortion:
- Fortune telling? "What's the actual probability?"
- Catastrophizing? "What's the most *likely* outcome, not the worst?"
- Emotional reasoning? "Just because I feel anxious doesn't mean the situation is dangerous."

## The Anxiety-Avoidance Feedback Loop

```
Uncertainty/Trigger
      ↓
Anxiety arises
      ↓
Worry (feels productive) ←── reinforces ──→ avoidance (feels safer)
      ↓                                        ↓
More anxiety                            Less competence/confidence
      ↓                                        ↓
More worry ←──────────────────────── More anxiety
```

**The exit:** Curiosity + committed action replace worry + avoidance. The loop unwinds.

---

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Fighting the anxiety.** Struggling against it strengthens it. See [[psychological-flexibility]] — make room, don't battle.
- **Seeking certainty.** Anxiety is the brain's response to uncertainty. You can't eliminate uncertainty. You can change your *relationship* to it.
- **Avoiding the trigger entirely.** Short-term relief, long-term amplification. Every avoidance teaches the brain "this is dangerous."
- **Intellectualizing.** "I know it's just a habit loop" ≠ feeling it. The shift happens experientially, not intellectually.
