# Situation: Managing Bottlenecks and Constraints

**When this applies:** A team, process, or organization is underperforming despite high activity levels — people are busy, machines are running, hours are being worked — but output is not improving. OR: you know intuitively there's a choke point but don't have a framework to diagnose it and act. Applies equally to manufacturing, software development, sales pipelines, and personal workflows.

## What the Wiki Recommends

You are experiencing the **local optimization trap**: improving individual parts of the system while ignoring the constraint that limits the whole. Goldratt's Theory of Constraints (TOC) provides the diagnostic and remediation framework. Until the bottleneck is identified and exploited, all other improvement work is wasted. Once it's identified, the five focusing steps are the only roadmap you need.

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Diagnose the Constraint

1. **Map the system** — write down every step from input to delivered output
2. **Find where work piles up** — inventory accumulates immediately upstream of a constraint
3. **Find where the next step waits** — downstream steps are idling while the constraint works
4. **Measure output rate at each step** — the slowest step is the bottleneck by definition
5. **Validate:** Does the system output match this bottleneck's output rate? (It should.)

Common constraint categories:
- **Physical bottleneck:** Machine capacity, physical workspace, equipment
- **Policy bottleneck:** A rule or approval process that only one person can authorize
- **People bottleneck:** Only one person has the knowledge or skill to proceed
- **Attention bottleneck (individual):** A leader whose decisions are required before anything moves

### Phase 2: Exploit the Constraint

Before adding resources, maximize what the constraint already produces:

- Eliminate non-value-adding work from the constraint's schedule (it should only do what only it can do)
- Remove idle time: if the constraint ever sits waiting for upstream feed, fix the upstream
- Improve quality at upstream steps to reduce rework arriving at the constraint
- Add a small buffer immediately upstream (the constraint must never idle)

> "Don't let the bottleneck sit idle. An hour lost there is an hour lost forever." — Goldratt

### Phase 3: Subordinate Everything Else

- Adjust upstream and downstream steps to produce exactly what the constraint needs — no more, no less
- Stop measuring non-constraint steps by utilization; measure them by whether they serve the constraint
- Resist management pressure to optimize non-constraint efficiency (it's a local optimization trap)

### Phase 4: Elevate If Needed

If exploitation is not sufficient, increase the constraint's capacity:
- Add headcount, machines, or tools at the bottleneck
- Break the approval monopoly (authorize multiple approvers)
- Invest in skill-building at the constraint

### Phase 5: Repeat

Once the current constraint is resolved, a new one will emerge. Return to step 1 immediately. This is the **ongoing improvement** cycle — not a one-time fix.

## Personal Application (Individual Bottleneck)

Tracy's ABCDE and Key Result Areas (see [[procrastination-framework]]) are personal TOC:

1. **Identify your personal constraint:** Which KRA, if weak, caps everything else?
2. **Exploit it:** Do the work that only you can do; protect your time for it
3. **Subordinate:** Everything else (email, low-value meetings, C tasks) serves your A task
4. **Elevate:** Invest in the skills your highest-leverage work requires

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Optimizing non-constraints:** Makes you busier without increasing output; builds inventory that costs money
- **Skipping exploitation (going straight to elevation):** Adding resources to an unexploited constraint rarely helps
- **Fixing the constraint and moving on:** The constraint shifts; continuous attention is required
- **Policy blindness:** The most common invisible constraint is a policy or approval process so normalized that no one questions it
