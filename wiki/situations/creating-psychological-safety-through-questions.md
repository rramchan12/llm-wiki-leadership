# Situation: Creating Psychological Safety Through Questions

**When this applies:** A team where people don't speak up, where bad news is slow to surface, where meetings produce agreement but hallway conversations reveal dissent, where no one challenges the leader's proposals — and you suspect the problem is culture, not personality. You want to shift from a silence-norm to a candor-norm.

## What the Wiki Recommends

The research is unambiguous (Edmondson; Schein): psychological safety is not a byproduct of team activities or trust-building exercises — it is created moment by moment through the **quality of listening and questioning** from leaders. The single most actionable lever is Humble Inquiry: the leader shifts from telling to genuinely asking, and from defending to genuinely receiving.

Safety is rebuilt from one interaction at a time. It cannot be declared or mandated; it can only be demonstrated consistently until people believe it's real.

## Step-by-Step Approach

### Phase 1: Diagnose the Safety Level

1. **Watch for silence signals:** Meeting agreement followed by private dissent; "it'll be fine" responses to genuine risk questions; volunteers only for low-stakes work
2. **Look at messenger history:** When was the last time someone brought you genuinely bad news? How did you respond?
3. **Ask directly (and carefully):** "I want to understand what makes it hard to bring problems to me." Then: listen without defending.

### Phase 2: Model the Behavior

4. **Admit what you don't know**
   - In the next meeting: "I don't have a clear answer on this. What are you seeing that I might be missing?"
   - The "one down" position signals that not-knowing is safe
   - See [[humble-inquiry]]

5. **Ask with genuine curiosity, not hidden agenda**
   - Pure Humble Inquiry: "Tell me what happened." Not: "Why did you make that choice?" (already positions for defense)
   - Hold the question open; do not finish people's sentences
   - Do not immediately problem-solve the answer they give

6. **Receive bad news visibly and well**
   - React to the unwelcome information with curiosity, not alarm or blame
   - "Thank you for telling me this" — and mean it
   - The most powerful safety signal is how you handle the first bad news that reaches you

7. **Acknowledge mistakes your own**
   - "I got that wrong. Here's what I should have done."
   - Leaders who admit mistakes lower the perceived cost of admission for everyone else

### Phase 3: Sustain the Pattern

8. **Follow up on what people say**
   - If someone raises a concern and nothing happens, silence learns that speaking is pointless
   - Close loops: "You raised X in our last meeting. Here's what we did about it."

9. **Protect the messenger in public**
   - When someone raises a difficult question in a meeting, support it: "That's exactly the right question."
   - Publicly reward candor; don't merely tolerate it

10. **Create structural safety opportunities**
    - Pre-mortems before major decisions (see [[premortem]]): everyone required to surface what could go wrong — removes the interpersonal risk of being "the one who raised the problem"
    - Retrospectives after projects: structured learning frame lowers the blame signal

### Phase 4: Calibrate at the Team Level

11. **Check for team jell conditions** (Peopleware)
    - Stable team membership, shared purpose, and protected work time create the relationship substrate in which safety grows
    - Fragmentation, competition, and chronic overtime destroy it
    - See [[team-jell]]

12. **Address the "punishing the messenger" pattern directly**
    - If there's a history of negative responses to bad news, name it: "I know I've responded to problems with [X]. That's been harmful. I'm committed to changing it."

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Asking but not listening:** Performative curiosity is immediately detected and destroys credibility
- **Asking once, then reverting:** People are watching to see if it's real; one reversion costs months of trust building
- **Confusing avoidance of conflict with safety:** A meeting with no disagreements is not safe — it may be dangerous
- **Safety theater:** Team activities ("safe space" declarations, team-building days) without behavioral change from leadership are counterproductive — they build cynicism
