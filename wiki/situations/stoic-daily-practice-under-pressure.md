# Situation: Applying Stoic Daily Practice Under Pressure

**When this applies:** You are in a period of sustained high-stakes pressure — a difficult leadership transition, organizational crisis, significant personal challenge, or any situation where reactivity is costly and clarity is hard to maintain. You want a Stoic practice framework that is practical and daily, not theoretical.

## What the Wiki Recommends

The Stoics — especially Marcus Aurelius (who was under genuine imperial-scale pressure) and Seneca (who was writing under threat of execution) — were not armchair philosophers. Their practice was built to function under maximum adversity. The Stoic toolkit is most powerful when used *before* pressure peaks (morning practice) and *during* it (in-the-moment interventions), not only when things have already gone wrong.

## Step-by-Step Daily Practice

### Morning (5–15 minutes)
1. **Premeditation ([[negative-visualization]]):** What difficulties might arise today? Run through 2–3 scenarios. You are not catastrophizing — you are pre-committing a response. If this happens, I will...
2. **[[dichotomy-of-control]] audit:** What is on today's agenda that I can influence vs. what I must accept? Write it out explicitly. Focus energy on the former.
3. **[[stoic-virtues]] set:** Pick one virtue as today's operating principle. *Justice* — am I treating everyone fairly today, including those with less power? *Courage* — am I avoiding a hard conversation or decision? *Moderation* — am I overreacting, overcontrolling? *Wisdom* — am I seeing second-order effects?
4. **View from above:** Briefly elevate perspective — in 10 years, what will matter from today's calendar?

### During the Day (Micro-interventions)
5. **Stimulus → space → response ([[dichotomy-of-control]]):** When triggered — anger, defensiveness, anxiety — recognize the space between what happened and your response. Even 3 seconds of deliberate pause is practice.
6. **"What does this situation require?" (Aurelius):** Rather than "what do I want?" ask what the situation genuinely calls for. This is the virtue check under fire.
7. **"Is this necessary?" (Aurelius):** Before any action or comment driven by ego, status, or emotion — pause and ask. This is the most useful word-count governor Aurelius offers.

### Evening (5 minutes)
8. **Self-examination (Seneca):** Three questions before sleep: What did I do wrong today? What did I do well? What could I improve tomorrow? This is not self-punishment — it is calibration.
9. **[[memento-mori]] reset:** One line: I had this day. It was finite. I am grateful it existed. Did I use it as if it mattered?

## The Character Compounding Principle

The Stoic case for daily practice is not just about managing pressure — it is about compounding character. Marcus Aurelius's insight: each small response to a small difficulty is a vote for who you are becoming. The leader who practices the pause before small provocations builds a reserve of equanimity for the large ones. *Character is formed in the repeated small moments, not salvaged in the dramatic large ones.*

## Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Treating Stoicism as suppression:** The goal is not to eliminate emotion but to choose your response to it. Stoics felt deeply; they were not cold.
- **The all-or-nothing trap:** An incomplete morning practice is better than none. 90 seconds of dichotomy-of-control reflection beats nothing.
- **Virtues as performance:** The check is internal. Performing virtue for an audience is not character practice.
- **Missing the social dimension:** Seneca's letters emphasize that Stoicism is practiced *in relationship* — with the people you lead, serve, and love. It is not a solo project.
