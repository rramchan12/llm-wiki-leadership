# Viktor E. Frankl (1905–1997)

**Role:** Austrian psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor; founder of logotherapy and existential analysis; author of *Man's Search for Meaning*.

## Why He Matters to This Wiki

Frankl provides the deepest available theoretical grounding for meaning under adversity. His work is not armchair philosophy — it emerged from three years in Nazi concentration camps (Auschwitz, Kaufering, Türkheim, Dachau), during which he tested and refined his theory that **meaning is the primary human motivational force** and that attitudinal freedom — the freedom to choose one's response — is the last freedom remaining when all others are stripped away.

His contribution to leadership thinking: you cannot sustain people through prolonged difficulty with incentives alone. Meaning must be present. And meaning can be found on three paths: creative contribution, experiential reception, and attitudinal choice in unavoidable suffering.

## Key Contributions

- **Logotherapy:** The therapeutic framework that meaning-seeking (not pleasure, not power) is the human primary drive. [[logotherapy]]
- **Tragic optimism:** The capacity to say "yes" to life despite the tragic triad of pain, guilt, and transience. [[tragic-optimism]]
- **The last human freedom:** Between stimulus and response lies the space to choose your response — available even in a concentration camp. Connects to [[dichotomy-of-control]].
- **Existential vacuum:** Diagnosed the epidemic of meaninglessness in affluent modern societies.
- **Noögenic neurosis:** A category of distress caused by meaninglessness, not psychodynamic conflict.

## Biography Notes

- Born Vienna, 1905. Trained under Alfred Adler, then diverged.
- Developed logotherapy in the 1930s, before imprisonment.
- Survived four camps; wife and most of his family did not.
- *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946) sold over 10 million copies; called by the Library of Congress one of the most influential books in America.
- Continued practicing psychiatry and lecturing into his 90s. Died 1997.
