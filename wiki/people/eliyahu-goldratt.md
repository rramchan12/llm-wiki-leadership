# Eliyahu M. Goldratt

**Role:** Israeli physicist turned management consultant; creator of the Theory of Constraints.

## Who He Is

Eliyahu Moshe Goldratt (1947–2011) was an Israeli physicist, business consultant, educator, and author. He studied physics at Tel Aviv University (undergraduate) and Bar-Ilan University (PhD). He founded Creative Output Inc. in the 1970s to commercialize scheduling software based on his early thinking about constraint management.

His turning point came when he realized the software was only as useful as the thinking behind it — and that thinking needed to be taught. He wrote *The Goal* (1984) as a business novel specifically to make the Theory of Constraints accessible and memorable. The book was originally rejected by multiple publishers before becoming a management classic.

## Key Works

- **The Goal** (1984, with Jeff Cox) — introduction of TOC through the story of Alex Rogo
- **It's Not Luck** (1994) — sequel to The Goal; TOC applied to marketing and strategy
- **The Theory of Constraints** (1990) — formal exposition of the TOC principles
- **Critical Chain** (1997) — TOC applied to project management
- **The Choice** (2008) — Goldratt's philosophical work on the nature of thinking clearly

## Core Ideas

- **The Goal is the Goal:** Every system must know explicitly what it is optimizing for
- **Theory of Constraints:** Identify, exploit, subordinate, elevate, repeat (see [[theory-of-constraints]])
- **Throughput, Inventory, Operating Expense:** The three measures that reveal systemic performance (see [[throughput-thinking]])
- **Socratic method:** Goldratt believed genuine understanding requires discovery; he never told, he asked

## Intellectual Legacy

Goldratt's TOC became a foundational framework in operations management, supply chain, and lean manufacturing. It also influenced project management (Critical Chain method), strategic planning, and eventually personal productivity (the local optimization trap applies to individual task management as well as factory floors).

His insistence on using novels as teaching vehicles was deliberately subversive: he believed business textbooks allowed readers to feel like they understood without actually internalizing the principles. A novel forces the reader to follow a thinking process in real time.
