# Tom DeMarco & Timothy Lister

**Role:** Software engineering consultants and authors; co-authors of *Peopleware* and foundational thinkers in software project management.

## Who They Are

**Tom DeMarco** is an American software engineer, author, and management consultant. He holds degrees from Cornell and Columbia and spent decades at Bell Labs before consulting. His early work on structured analysis (*Structured Analysis and System Specification*, 1978) made him a respected technical voice; *Peopleware* shifted that voice entirely to the human and organizational side.

**Timothy Lister** is DeMarco's long-time colleague and partner at The Atlantic Systems Guild, their consulting practice. Together they conducted the Coding War Games — one of the most cited empirical studies in software productivity research — and wrote *Peopleware* in 1987. A revised edition appeared in 1999; a third edition in 2013.

## Key Works

- **Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams** (1987/1999/2013) — definitive treatise on the human factors in software development
- **Waltzing with Bears** (2003, DeMarco & Lister) — risk management in software projects
- **The Deadline** (1997, DeMarco) — novel about software project management
- **Slack** (2001, DeMarco) — argument that organizations need slack time for innovation and recovery

## Core Ideas

- **Sociology matters more than technology:** The real problems of knowledge work are human, not technical
- **Flow and E-factor:** Knowledge workers need uninterrupted time; the E-factor (environmental) is the key productivity variable
- **Team jell:** The conditions for genuine team cohesion and their systematic destruction by common management practices (see [[team-jell]])
- **Manager's role:** Create conditions for people to work effectively; don't manage the work, manage the environment

## Intellectual Context

DeMarco and Lister were writing in the era of the first open-plan office wave — when management consultants were arguing that cubicle farms and matrix organizations would improve collaboration. *Peopleware* was a principled counter-argument grounded in observation: productivity data showed the best performers worked in better physical conditions and on more stable teams.

Three decades later, the rise and fall of the open office proves them right.
