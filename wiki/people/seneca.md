# Seneca (Lucius Annaeus Seneca, c. 4 BC – 65 AD)

**Role:** Roman Stoic philosopher, statesman, playwright, and advisor to Emperor Nero. Author of *Letters from a Stoic* (Epistulae Morales), *On the Shortness of Life*, *On Benefits*, and numerous other essays and tragedies.

## Why He Matters to This Wiki

Seneca is the most *readable* and *daily-applicable* of the classical Stoics. Where Marcus Aurelius wrote private notes to himself and Epictetus taught through a disciple's transcript, Seneca wrote in epistolary form — *as if to a friend* — making his philosophy feel personal and immediate rather than academic. His recurring subjects (time, death, wealth, friendship, busyness, virtue) are exactly the subjects that leadership and life-design thinking grapples with today.

His most powerful contributions: the ferocity of his claim that **time is the only thing we own** and the practical accessibility of his death-acceptance framework.

## Key Contributions

- **Time as singular possession:** *"Omnia, Lucili, aliena sunt, tempus tantum nostrum est."* All things are foreign; time alone is ours. [[memento-mori]]
- **Premeditatio malorum:** Pre-experiencing loss to reduce attachment and prepare response. [[negative-visualization]]
- **Virtue as the only true good:** External goods are preferred indifferents; only virtue is unconditional. [[stoic-virtues]]
- **The examined life must be practiced, not professed:** Philosophy is the medicine of life, daily taken, not a credential. [[stoic-daily-practice-under-pressure]]
- **Stillness over travel:** Restless movement (peregrinatio) changes landscape, not mind. See [[aurelius-meditations]] for parallel.

## Biography Notes

- Born in Córdoba (Roman Hispania), c. 4 BC.
- Educated and rose to prominence in Rome; became one of the leading orators of his time.
- Exiled to Corsica by Emperor Claudius (41–49 AD) — returned to tutor the future emperor Nero.
- Served as Nero's advisor; amassed considerable wealth; the contradiction between his philosophy of simplicity and his wealth was noted by contemporaries.
- Compelled to commit suicide by Nero in 65 AD, after alleged involvement in the Pisonian conspiracy. Died Stoically, by all accounts.
- The letters to Lucilius were written in his final years under these dangerous conditions.
